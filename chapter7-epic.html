1 The Founding Story: A Patient Dies, and a Database Is Born

In the mid-1970s, Gordon Faulkner, a pediatrician in Madison, Wisconsin, learned that one of his young patients had died. The child’s family had moved to Milwaukee, 75 miles away. When the child fell ill at a new hospital, the physicians there had no access to her medical history. They did not know her conditions. They did not know how to treat her. Gordon believed, quietly and with certainty, that if those doctors had had her records, she would have survived.

His wife, Judy Faulkner, was a graduate student in computer science at the University of Wisconsin. She had taken one of the first courses ever offered on computers in medicine, under a visionary professor named Warner Slack. The morning after hearing about the child, she went back to work with a new sense of urgency. She had already been building a database for tracking patient information over time. It was no longer just a research project. It was a mission.

The founding idea — 1979
“Patient information should follow the patient — not stay locked in a filing cabinet at the last doctor’s office they visited.”

In 1979, working on a single refrigerator-sized minicomputer in the basement of a house at 2020 University Avenue in Madison, Judy Faulkner co-founded a company called Human Services Computing with $70,000 in startup capital. The company would later be renamed Epic Systems. She has run it ever since, now in her eighties, overseeing a company of 14,000 employees on a 1,670-acre campus outside Madison.

Epic has never gone public. It has never taken outside investment after those early days. It has never made a significant acquisition. Faulkner runs it according to principles she calls “commandments” painted on walls throughout the campus. The first three: do not go public, do not acquire or be acquired, and software must work. This independence has allowed Epic to make long-term decisions that publicly traded companies cannot — including investing decades in data infrastructure that now makes its entire AI strategy possible.

2 Twenty Years of Paper: What Healthcare Looked Like Before the EHR

To understand Epic’s story, you have to understand what healthcare looked like before it. For most of the twentieth century, and well into the twenty-first, the medical record was a physical object: a manila folder stuffed with handwritten notes, carbon-copy prescription pads, typed discharge summaries, and stapled lab printouts. It lived in a filing cabinet at a single hospital or clinic. When a patient went to a different facility, their records did not go with them.

The practical consequences were severe. Physicians made decisions without a patient’s full history. Medications were prescribed without knowing what a patient was already taking elsewhere. Allergies discovered at one hospital were unknown at another. Redundant tests were ordered because no one knew the same tests had already been run. Specialists wrote letters to referring physicians that arrived weeks after the visit. Emergency physicians made life-or-death decisions with incomplete information as a matter of routine.

What paper looked like in practice
A typical patient encounter in 1995

A 67-year-old woman arrives at an emergency department in severe abdominal pain. She takes medications for heart disease but cannot remember their names. Her primary care doctor is in a different health system. His office won’t open for three hours. The hospital has no record of her previous visits because she was last hospitalized at a different facility five years ago.

The ER physician orders a full medication reconciliation from scratch. Blood is drawn for labs that were already done at her cardiologist’s office two weeks ago. She waits. The physician makes decisions based on what the patient can remember, supplemented by educated guesses. This is not a worst-case scenario. This is Tuesday.

Epic’s earliest products addressed this problem on a small scale through the 1980s and 1990s. But adoption was slow and expensive. Most hospitals remained on paper, or on fragmented electronic systems that could not communicate with each other. A patient’s information was still trapped — just now in a digital silo instead of a filing cabinet.

3 The Digital Mandate: HITECH, Meaningful Use, and the Great Digitization (2009–2015)

The moment that changed everything was not a technology breakthrough. It was a law. In February 2009, President Obama signed the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act. Buried inside it was the Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health Act — HITECH.

HITECH created a simple but powerful incentive: hospitals and doctors who switched to certified electronic health records and actually used them would receive large bonus payments from the government’s Medicare and Medicaid programs (the insurance programs that cover seniors, people with disabilities, and low-income Americans). Those who had not made the switch by 2015 would start losing money through penalties. The federal government committed $27 billion to the program. The message to healthcare was unmistakable: go digital now, or pay the price.

Key concept
Meaningful Use
Meaningful Use was the checklist hospitals and doctors had to complete to get their HITECH bonus payments. It was not enough to just buy the software — you had to prove you were actually using it in ways that helped patients. That meant things like sending prescriptions electronically instead of handwriting them, sharing patient information with other providers digitally, letting patients see their own records through an online portal, and tracking whether care was actually getting better over time. Meaningful Use set a minimum standard for what EHR systems had to do, which forced the entire industry to get on roughly the same page. The program has since been renamed “Promoting Interoperability,” but it still shapes how EHRs work today.

The results were dramatic. In 2008, fewer than 10% of hospitals used electronic health records. By 2015, more than 80% did. The U.S. healthcare system went digital faster than almost any other industry in American history.

Epic was the biggest winner. Its software did everything hospitals needed in one package — clinical notes, billing, lab orders, pharmacy, and the patient portal (MyChart) — which made it the easiest way to check all the Meaningful Use boxes. Between 2009 and 2015, Epic went from serving hundreds of hospitals to thousands. Hospitals were waiting years just to get Epic installed. The company’s campus in Verona, Wisconsin, kept expanding to keep up.

The policy foundation for AI — connecting the dots
Here is the chain of events that makes AI in healthcare possible today: the HITECH Act (2009) paid hospitals to go digital. The Affordable Care Act (2010) — also known as Obamacare — changed how hospitals get paid: instead of just getting paid for each visit, hospitals started getting rewarded for keeping patients healthy and penalized when patients kept coming back to the hospital. That meant hospitals suddenly needed data — not just to write down what happened during a visit, but to track whether patients were actually getting better over time. Together, these two laws pushed American healthcare to go digital and gave hospitals a reason to collect detailed patient data year after year. That data became Cosmos. Cosmos became the training ground for AI. And today, 85% of Epic’s customers are actively using AI tools that could not exist without the digital records those laws created. Nobody writing HITECH in 2009 was thinking about AI agents. But they were building the foundation for them — one hospital, one patient record, one data point at a time.

4 The Unintended Consequence: When Digital Created New Burdens

The digitization of American healthcare solved the access problem. Patient information could now follow the patient — at least within systems using the same software. But it created a problem no one fully anticipated: documentation burden.

Paper records were incomplete and messy — but they were fast. A doctor could scribble a few lines of notes, sign the chart, and move on to the next patient. Electronic health records were thorough and organized, but they demanded far more typing. Every medication had to be picked from a dropdown menu. Every diagnosis had to be entered using a specific code. Every clinical decision had to be spelled out in a structured form. Every interaction with an insurance company created more paperwork. The EHR was supposed to make information easier to access — and it did — but it also made creating that information much more work.

The numbers behind the crisis
By 2023, the American Medical Association found that 48% of physicians reported feeling burned out, with EHR documentation consistently identified as the leading cause. Physicians reported spending an average of nine minutes in the EHR for every 15 minutes spent with a patient. More than 20% reported spending over eight hours per week on the EHR outside of normal working hours — a phenomenon the medical community calls “pajama time.” Emergency department nurses spent approximately 27% of their shifts on EHR-related tasks — nearly the same proportion as direct patient care. The technology designed to improve healthcare was, in measurable and documented ways, degrading the experience of practicing it.

This created a painful irony at the heart of Epic’s success. Epic’s software was both the backbone of modern healthcare and a major cause of doctor and nurse burnout. The company whose whole mission was to help patients had built a system that was, in many cases, pulling doctors and nurses away from the people they were trying to help.

👨‍⚕️   Physician perspective
“I went into medicine to take care of patients. By 2018, I was spending more time staring at a computer screen than looking at the person in front of me. I knew my patient’s name, but my eyes were on the dropdown menu for their medication. I had become a data entry specialist with a medical degree.” — Composite of reported experiences from AMA physician surveys, 2022–2024
👩‍⚕️   Nurse perspective
“We were hired to care for patients, but half my shift is clicking through screens. Medication administration requires multiple clicks to document each dose. Vital signs have to be entered in several places. I love my patients — but the EHR makes me feel like the patient is the computer, not the person in the bed.” — Composite of reported nursing experiences

This is the context into which Epic began deploying AI. The problem was not primarily a data problem. It was a human problem: how do you give clinicians back the time and attention that digitization had, unintentionally, taken from them?

5 The Data Foundation: Cosmos and Why 300 Million Records Matter

Before we can talk about the AI, we need to understand what made it possible: the data. Over forty-five years — long before anyone was talking about ChatGPT or AI agents — Epic quietly built the largest collection of patient health data ever assembled.

Cosmos is Epic’s giant, anonymized patient data platform. When hospitals that use Epic agree to participate, their patient data (with names and identifying information removed) flows into a shared research database. Today, Cosmos holds records from more than 300 million patients and more than 16 billion individual data points: diagnoses, medications, lab results, vital signs, scan results, doctor’s notes, procedures, and outcomes — collected over time, across different hospitals and clinics, in a consistent digital format. The Affordable Care Act helped Cosmos grow faster because it pushed hospitals to track patient outcomes over the long term — which generated exactly the kind of detailed, ongoing data that AI systems need to learn from.

Key concept
Longitudinal clinical data
Most data is a snapshot — it tells you what is true at one moment. Longitudinal data tells a story over time: what happened to this patient over months or years, across different doctors and hospitals. This is what makes Cosmos so powerful for training AI. An AI trained on Cosmos does not just learn what a patient looks like at a single visit — it learns what tends to happen next for patients like this one. Who gets better? Who gets worse? Which treatments work? Which warning signs show up before a crisis? You cannot learn those patterns from a single snapshot, because medicine plays out over time.

Here is a concrete example of what this data makes possible: Epic’s Sepsis Prediction Model. Sepsis is a life-threatening condition where the body’s response to an infection starts damaging its own organs — it kills hundreds of thousands of Americans each year, and catching it early is the difference between life and death. Epic’s AI monitors every hospitalized patient at participating hospitals every 15 minutes, recalculating each patient’s risk of sepsis using dozens of measurements updated in real time. At one hospital system, sepsis-related deaths dropped by 44% after the model was turned on. A separate study found a 17% reduction in deaths. These are not small improvements in efficiency. These are people who are alive because an AI caught something early enough to save them. And it only works because of the data foundation that was built over decades before anyone was thinking about AI.

6 The AI Era Begins: From Data Storage to Intelligence

Epic’s AI journey did not start with ChatGPT-style AI. It began years earlier with predictive models — software trained to spot patterns in patient data and warn doctors about risk before it turned into a crisis.

AI as early warning system
Epic deploys machine learning models trained on Cosmos data. The Sepsis Model, deterioration index, and readmission risk tools run in the background of every Epic installation, alerting nurses and physicians when risk crosses a threshold. AI begins to write

2024–2026 — The agentic era
AI begins to act

Each phase built on the previous one. Predictive models required Cosmos. Generative AI required the EHR integrations that Meaningful Use compliance had forced hospitals to build. Agentic AI requires the institutional trust that years of predictive and generative AI deployment have established. This is not a sudden transformation. It is the culmination of a forty-five-year compounding investment.

7 Ambient AI: DAX Copilot and the Doctor-Patient Relationship

The most immediately impactful AI deployment Epic has made is also the most human: a system designed to give doctors back the ability to look their patients in the eye.

DAX Copilot (Dragon Ambient eXperience Copilot) is built through a partnership between Epic, Microsoft, and Nuance. A physician opens Epic on a tablet, taps a button to begin a visit, and DAX Copilot activates. It listens to the conversation between the physician and patient — not recording a transcript, but understanding the clinical meaning of what is being said in real time, using large language models fine-tuned on clinical language. When the visit ends, DAX Copilot produces a complete, structured draft clinical note within seconds. The physician reads it, edits anything that needs correction, and approves it for the medical record. Documentation is done before the patient leaves the room.

Key concept
Ambient AI
Ambient AI refers to artificial intelligence that operates passively in the background of an environment, gathering information and generating outputs without requiring explicit prompts or commands from the user. The physician does not type a query. They do not dictate notes. They simply conduct the visit naturally, and the AI observes, understands, and acts. The word “ambient” is important: it means the AI is present without demanding attention — woven into the environment rather than imposed on it. This is the opposite of the EHR workflow it is replacing, which demanded physician attention constantly: menus to navigate, fields to fill, codes to select. Ambient AI asks for nothing from the clinician during the visit. It gives something back afterward: time.

The measured results are striking. Clinicians using DAX report a 50% reduction in documentation time, 70% reduction in feelings of burnout and fatigue, an average of seven minutes saved per patient encounter, and the ability to see an additional five patients per day. At Northwestern Medicine, physicians using DAX in at least half their encounters were seeing an additional 11.3 patients per month. At WellSpan Health, 94% of physicians reported that DAX improved the quality of their patient interactions.

But the most important finding is not in the productivity data. When physicians are not typing, they look at their patients. That shift — from screen to face — changes the encounter in ways that are measurable in patient satisfaction scores but felt most directly in the examination room.

👨‍⚕️   Physician using DAX Copilot
“For the first time in years, I was actually present. Not multitasking between the patient and the keyboard. I could make eye contact. I could listen to what they were actually saying, not just what I needed to document. One of my patients asked me what was different. I told her I got a little help with the paperwork. She said, ‘You seem like yourself again.’”
🧑‍🦳   Patient perspective
“I used to feel like the doctor was only half listening — typing the whole time. Now they look at me. They ask follow-up questions. I feel like I’m talking to a human being again, not filling out a form. I don’t know what changed, but something did.” — Composite of patient experience reports from DAX deployment studies

In late 2025, Microsoft extended ambient AI to nurses with Dragon Copilot for nurses — designed specifically around nursing documentation workflows, which historically had been poorly served by existing AI tools. Emergency department nurses who spend roughly a quarter of their shift on EHR tasks are primary beneficiaries. The principle is the same: capture documentation passively while the nurse cares for the patient, rather than demanding documentation at the expense of care.

In early 2026, Epic launched Chart with Art — built-in AI charting that listens during patient visits, drafts the clinician’s note, and queues up orders based on the conversation. After launching across multiple outpatient specialties, Houston Methodist became the first organization to use Chart with Art for bedside nursing workflows, with home care workflows following shortly after. Nurses using Art’s end-of-shift note drafting are writing notes 85% faster. At Riverside Health in Virginia, clinicians using Art’s Inpatient Insights are spending up to 32% less time on documentation and communication tasks.

2026 update — Art moves beyond documentation
Art is no longer just a documentation tool. At The Christ Hospital in Ohio, Art extracts incidental findings from routine radiology results and drives follow-up, delivering an early detection rate of 69% for lung cancer versus the national average of 46%. This is a qualitative shift: the AI is not merely writing notes faster. It is surfacing clinical signals that might otherwise go unnoticed — and connecting those signals to action. More than 100 cases of lung cancer were detected earlier as a result. The difference between “faster documentation” and “earlier diagnosis” is the difference between efficiency and clinical impact.

Conversational AI in the exam room

The next step in ambient AI is a shift from passive listening to active participation. Epic is developing conversational AI for clinicians that goes beyond drafting notes: during the patient visit, Art will surface answers to questions that the clinician and patient ask in real time. A physician can say, “Art, how is this patient’s blood pressure trending?” and receive an immediate answer drawn from the full chart — clinical notes, lab results, medications, imaging, and billing data. Art will also respond to inferred questions: if a patient expresses frustration with their current treatment, Art can surface data-driven alternative options without being asked.

This is a fundamental change in the physician-AI relationship. In the ambient model, the AI listens and writes. In the conversational model, the AI participates in the clinical encounter as a real-time data assistant — instantly pulling information from across the enterprise that a physician would otherwise spend minutes hunting for in the chart. The physician stays in the conversation with the patient. Art does the chart review in the background.

Key concept
Collaborative visit agendas
One of the most promising near-term features is the collaborative visit agenda, where Art and Emmie work together before and during the appointment. Before the visit, Emmie reaches out to the patient through MyChart: “What do you want to discuss at your appointment? What symptoms have changed? What questions do you want your doctor to answer?” Emmie gathers the patient’s priorities and combines them with Art’s clinical analysis of what matters in the chart — overdue screenings, trending lab values, medication changes since the last visit. The result is a single, shared agenda that both patient and clinician can see before the visit begins. During the visit, Art monitors the conversation and helps the physician pace through all the agenda items. After the visit, Emmie follows up with the patient: explaining test results in plain language, providing medication reminders, and answering questions. Two AI agents, working in coordination, bookend the entire clinical encounter.

8 Agentic AI: When AI Stops Answering and Starts Acting

Ambient AI listens and generates. It produces a draft note that a human reviews. It is powerful, but it is still a tool that responds to input and requires human action to produce an outcome in the medical record. Agentic AI is categorically different.

At HIMSS 2025, Epic’s VP of R&D Seth Howard described the shift directly: “We’ve woven AI into the foundational capabilities of Epic, and we’ve been working towards an agentic platform for the past year or so. We’re really building on the foundation that we created to have generative AI as part of the software to start building reusable components that can take action, under human oversight.”

Key concept
Agentic AI
Think about the difference between asking someone a question and giving someone a job. When you ask ChatGPT to write an email, it does one thing and gives you the result. That is standard AI. An agentic AI is different: you give it a goal, and it figures out the steps on its own. It plans what to do, uses whatever tools it needs (databases, scheduling systems, messaging apps), checks its own work, and adjusts if something does not go as expected. In healthcare, a standard AI writes a note when a doctor asks for one. An agentic AI might review a patient’s chart on its own, notice that a diabetes screening was missed, draft a personalized message to the patient, suggest scheduling a follow-up appointment, notify the care coordinator, and flag the case for a nurse — all without anyone asking it to do any of those things individually. The key difference is that an agentic AI can handle a multi-step process on its own, not just respond to a single request.

This distinction matters a lot in healthcare, because the stakes of letting AI act on its own are higher here than in almost any other industry. Every previous chapter in this book looked at AI that makes predictions or recommendations. Uber’s algorithm predicts demand and sets prices automatically. Spotify’s system picks songs for your playlist. In those cases, the prediction and the action are tightly linked — the AI decides and acts in the same moment. Agentic AI is different because it can work toward a complex, multi-step goal that requires thinking, using tools, and adjusting along the way — not just producing a single answer.

And that is exactly why the phrase “under human oversight” in Howard’s statement is not just legal fine print. It is the central question of Epic’s entire AI program. The AI is already capable enough to act on its own for many tasks. The real question is: how accurate does it need to be, and for which specific tasks, before it is safe to let it act without a human double-checking every single thing it does?

9 Epic’s Agents: Art, Penny, and Emmie

Epic is not building one agentic AI. It is building a fleet of specialized agents, each designed for a specific clinical or administrative workflow. Think of them less as a single AI assistant and more as a team of invisible staff members — each with a defined role, operating within defined constraints, always surfacing their work to a human before it takes effect. As of 2026, this fleet has three named members — Art for clinicians, Penny for the back office, and Emmie for patients.

Art: The Clinical Intelligence Agent

Art is Epic’s AI assistant for doctors and nurses. Before a doctor walks into an exam room, Art does the homework. It reads through the patient’s entire medical history in Epic, pulls out the most important information, and creates a short summary: the key diagnoses, any medication changes since the last visit, lab results that are trending in the wrong direction, and health screenings the patient is overdue for. For complicated cases, Art can search across all 16+ billion data points in Cosmos to find similar patients and what happened to them — answering questions like “What usually works for patients with this combination of problems?”

Art in practice
The two-minute pre-brief that used to take ten

A primary care doctor has not seen a patient in 18 months. Without Art, she would spend the first several minutes of the appointment scrolling through the digital chart, trying to piece together what happened since the last visit: which medications changed, what notes came in from specialists, which test results need attention. It is not unusual for this chart-review to eat up a third of a 20-minute appointment — time that could have been spent talking to the patient.

With Art, she opens the chart and reads a focused summary: two ongoing conditions, one medication change made by a heart doctor, a kidney function number that is creeping up and nobody has addressed yet, and a mammogram that is two years overdue. She walks into the room already knowing what matters. The patient gets more attention in the same amount of time.

Penny: The Revenue Cycle Agent

Penny handles the part of healthcare that patients rarely see: the fight over money between hospitals and insurance companies. When an insurance company refuses to pay for a treatment — which happens about 17% of the time — a human staff member traditionally has to research the denial, dig through the patient’s medical records for evidence, and write an appeal letter from scratch. This takes 45 minutes or more per denial, and many denials just go unchallenged because the cost of fighting them is higher than what the hospital would get back.

Penny does this work automatically: it reads the denial, pulls the relevant medical records from the patient’s chart, finds the right insurance policy language, writes the appeal letter, and hands it to an administrator to review before sending. The human checks it over and hits submit. The whole thing takes minutes instead of nearly an hour — which means hospitals can now fight denials that used to not be worth the effort. At Summit Health, Penny cut the time to submit medication approvals by 42%, and 92% of its AI-written responses were accepted without any changes. Across hospitals using Penny the most, billing errors that led to denied claims dropped by more than 20%, and appeal letters are being written 23% faster. Over time, Penny will handle more of this on its own — completing some tasks from start to finish within whatever safety rules each hospital sets up.

Why the administrative agent matters for patient care
Insurance claim denials cost U.S. hospitals an estimated $19.7 billion per year just in the cost of processing them. When the cost of fighting a denial drops from 45 minutes of staff time to 3 minutes of review, hospitals can fight denials they used to just accept as losses. That recovered money pays for nurses, equipment, and expanded services. AI that does boring paperwork in the back office has a direct impact on what is possible at the patient’s bedside. This is not an abstract connection — it is how healthcare economics actually works.

Emmie: The Patient-Facing Agent

Emmie is Epic’s AI for patients — the agent that lives inside MyChart, the patient portal used by hundreds of millions of Americans. Emmie handles the interactions patients have with the health system between visits: answering billing questions, helping patients schedule and reschedule appointments, sending follow-up reminders about care plans, and increasingly, answering health questions within the context of the patient’s own medical record.

The measured results are significant. At Rush University Medical Center, Emmie delivered a sustained 58% reduction in billing-related customer service messages, freeing staff to focus on complex cases while patients get immediate answers to straightforward questions. At Ochsner Health, patients rescheduled more than 14,900 appointments through Emmie, saving nearly 750 hours of staff time. Patients using Emmie for post-visit follow-up reminders report a 94% satisfaction rate.

2026 update — Ask Emmie launches
Sutter Health became the first hospital system to go live with Ask Emmie, an AI chatbot inside MyChart that answers patients’ health questions using their own medical record. Unlike a generic chatbot that gives the same answers to everyone, Ask Emmie knows what medications you take, what diagnoses you have, and what test results you just got — and responds based on all of that. This is part of a bigger pattern: AI is moving from storing your health information (the EHR), to finding information when asked (search), to understanding your information and explaining it to you in plain language (Ask Emmie). That shift from looking things up to actually understanding them is what makes this generation of AI fundamentally different.
Art, Penny, Emmie: the complete loop
Notice how the three agents form a complete system: Art works with clinicians during and around the visit. Penny handles the financial and administrative infrastructure behind the visit. Emmie works with patients before and after the visit. Together, they cover every major stakeholder and every phase of the care cycle. This is not a coincidence — it is platform strategy. By building AI agents for every participant in the healthcare interaction, Epic is ensuring that the value generated at each step feeds back into the system as training data for the others. A patient’s question to Emmie reveals a care gap that Art surfaces for the physician, whose response generates documentation that helps Penny ensure proper reimbursement. The flywheel is not just data → model → prediction. It is patient → clinician → administrator → patient, with AI accelerating every handoff.

10 The Platform: Data Gravity, Agent Factory, and Curiosity

Art, Penny, and Emmie are Epic’s AI agents. But to understand why they matter strategically — and why no competitor can easily replicate them — you have to understand the platform underneath them. This section is about power: who controls the data, who controls the AI, and what that means for everyone else.

MyChart, Data Gravity, and Epic as the Gatekeeper of Healthcare AI

There is a structural reality underneath all of Epic’s AI strategy that a business student should understand clearly: Epic controls the interface through which most Americans interact with their healthcare data. MyChart — the patient portal embedded in every Epic installation — is used by more than 250 million people. Roughly 78% of American healthcare data flows through Epic’s EHR platform. Epic serves more than 2,000 healthcare organizations, including 27 of the 31 U.S. News Top Hospitals. Any AI product that wants to operate at the point of care in the American healthcare system must, in most cases, integrate with Epic.

This is a concept called data gravity: once data accumulates in a platform at sufficient scale, it becomes increasingly difficult and expensive to move it elsewhere. Applications, services, and AI models are pulled toward the data, not the other way around. Epic’s 45 years of EHR installations created a gravitational field so strong that even the largest technology companies in the world — Microsoft, Google, Amazon — must build on top of Epic rather than around it. Microsoft’s DAX Copilot does not replace Epic. It plugs into Epic. Google Health cannot train on Epic’s data without Epic’s permission. Third-party AI vendors must pass through Epic’s App Orchard marketplace and meet its security and integration review before their products can touch patient data in an Epic system.

Key concept
Data gravity
Data gravity is the idea that once a huge amount of data piles up in one place, everything else gets pulled toward it — because it is easier to bring new tools to the data than to move all that data somewhere else. Think of it like a planet: the bigger it gets, the stronger its gravitational pull. Epic’s EHR holds decades of patient records in organized, digital formats. AI needs this data to learn. Hospitals need this data to operate. Patients need this data to manage their health. And realistically, all of that data cannot just be picked up and moved to a competitor — it is too massive, too legally complicated, and the trust relationships that built it took decades to develop. This gives Epic enormous power as the gatekeeper of healthcare AI: Epic gets to decide which AI tools can access the data, under what rules, and through what channels.

This gatekeeper position has become controversial. In 2025, the Texas Attorney General sued Epic, accusing the company of using its dominant market position to block competitors from accessing patient data — a type of lawsuit called an “antitrust” case, where the government argues a company has too much power and is using it unfairly. A separate group lawsuit claims that Epic’s MyChart system makes it difficult for patients who see multiple doctors to pull their complete medical records together in one place. Epic has pushed back on both claims, pointing out that it handles over 725 million record exchanges per month, works with nearly 1,000 patient apps, and has shared data with the Social Security Administration for 15 years. These legal fights are still playing out, but the business reality is clear: when one company controls where the data lives, it also controls which AI gets built, by whom, and for whose benefit.

The gatekeeper question for students
Epic’s control over healthcare data is both a huge advantage for AI quality — because having all the data in one organized place makes for better AI — and a potential problem for competition and patient choice. If one company controls the software, the data, and now the AI models (through Curiosity), what stops that company from putting its own business interests ahead of patients? This is not a hypothetical question. It is the same tension you see whenever a single company dominates a platform — think of the debates about Apple’s App Store or Amazon’s marketplace — except the stakes are people’s health.

Agent Factory: When Health Systems Build Their Own Agents

At HIMSS 2026, Epic announced something that changes the competitive dynamics of healthcare AI entirely: Agent Factory, a fully integrated platform that lets health systems create, customize, and deploy their own AI agents — without writing code.

Key concept
Agent Factory
Agent Factory is Epic’s visual builder for creating custom AI agents. Health systems can equip agents with their own local policies and knowledge bases, define the workflows those agents operate within, specify the guardrails and approval checkpoints, and deploy them on their own timeline. Agent actions are fully traceable — every step an agent takes is logged and auditable. This is a platform play: instead of only using Epic’s pre-built agents (Art, Penny, Emmie), health systems can now build agents tailored to their own specific operational needs. A rural hospital might build an agent for patient transportation coordination. An academic medical center might build one for clinical trial matching. The shift from “use our agents” to “build your own agents on our platform” mirrors exactly what happened when Apple launched the App Store — the platform became more valuable than any single application on it.

Agent Factory represents Epic’s bet that the most valuable healthcare AI will not be the agents Epic builds centrally, but the thousands of agents that individual health systems build for their own unique contexts. Epic provides the platform, the data infrastructure, the safety guardrails, and the integration with the EHR. Health systems provide the clinical and operational knowledge of what their patients and staff actually need. This is the same platform-and-ecosystem strategy that made Apple, Salesforce, and Uber dominant in their respective markets — and it only works because HITECH forced the data into Epic’s systems in the first place.

Curiosity: Epic’s Own Medical Foundation Models

Perhaps the most ambitious development in Epic’s AI strategy is Curiosity — a new family of medical foundation models that Epic is training on its own data, rather than relying solely on general-purpose models from Microsoft and OpenAI.

Key concept
Curiosity (medical foundation model)
Curiosity is a family of AI models that Epic built and trained using real (but anonymized) patient data from more than 300 million people in Cosmos. Here is why that matters: most AI models you hear about (like GPT-4 or Claude) learned by reading text from the internet and were then adapted for medical use. Curiosity is different — it was trained directly on actual medical events: real diagnoses, real medications, real lab results, real procedures, and real outcomes. Because of this, Curiosity does not just understand the words doctors use. It understands the patterns of what actually happens to patients over time — the next likely diagnosis, the next medication change, the next risk of being hospitalized. Early testing shows Curiosity performs as well as or better than specialized medical AI tools at predicting things like how long a patient will stay in the hospital, whether they will be readmitted, and what diseases they might develop. The difference in plain terms: a regular AI knows the language of medicine. Curiosity knows the story of medicine — what actually happens to real people.

Curiosity became available to researchers in the Cosmos community in February 2026, with visualization tools that let researchers explore projected patient trajectories. Epic’s chief medical officer, Dr. Jackie Gerhart, described the potential: with Curiosity, clinicians could take many real-world patient journeys and apply them to the patient sitting in front of them, predicting the most likely next medical event. The implications include shorter time to diagnosis, shorter time to treatment, and potentially keeping patients out of the emergency room entirely.

Why Curiosity changes the competitive landscape
When Epic was using only Microsoft’s AI models, it was essentially a customer — buying someone else’s technology. With Curiosity, Epic becomes an AI company itself. This is a big strategic deal: Epic now controls both the data (Cosmos) and the AI models trained on that data. No other healthcare company has both. Google cannot train models on Cosmos data. Neither can Amazon or any startup. The data advantage that decades of EHR adoption created is now being turned directly into an AI advantage. This is the end result of the entire story this chapter tells: the company that digitized the most patient records now builds the most powerful medical AI using those records.

12 The Human-in-the-Loop Question

Every AI system in this chapter keeps a human in the loop at the decision stage. This is intentional — and it will not stay this way forever. Understanding why this design choice was made, and how it will evolve, is one of the most important things this chapter can teach.

Key concept
Human-in-the-loop vs. human-on-the-loop
These terms describe two different ways humans and AI can work together. Human-in-the-loop means a human must personally review and approve every single thing the AI produces before it counts. The doctor who reads the AI-drafted note before it goes into the patient’s chart is “in the loop.” Human-on-the-loop means the AI acts on its own while a human watches from a higher level and can step in if something goes wrong. An administrator who checks a dashboard of AI-submitted insurance appeals once a day, instead of approving each letter one by one, is “on the loop.” As AI systems prove themselves accurate across millions of uses, and as regulations and trust catch up, organizations will slowly move from in-the-loop to on-the-loop for certain tasks. The key question is not whether this shift will happen, but which tasks, at what level of proven accuracy, and with what rules and oversight in place.
The stakes of getting this wrong
AI mistakes in healthcare are not like AI mistakes anywhere else. If Spotify recommends a song you hate, you skip it — no harm done. If an AI gets a medication dose wrong, misses an allergy, or fails to flag a patient who is getting sicker, the consequences can be permanent and irreversible. This is why moving from human-in-the-loop to human-on-the-loop is so much slower, so much more careful, and so much more heavily regulated in healthcare than in other industries. The question is not whether AI can be accurate. The question is: how accurate does it have to be, proven across how many patients, before we stop checking every single thing it does? And who takes the blame when that judgment turns out to be wrong?

13 The Risks: Alert Fatigue, Deskilling, and Clinician Resistance

The benefits of healthcare AI are real and measurable. But so are the risks — and some of the most important ones have nothing to do with whether the AI is accurate.

Alert fatigue is the first major risk. When AI systems send too many notifications — too many risk scores, too many reminders about screenings, too many suggested actions — doctors stop reading them. It is like getting so many phone notifications that you start ignoring all of them. Epic’s sepsis model ran into exactly this problem at some hospitals: the alerts came so often that doctors started dismissing them without looking. The model was accurate, but it did not matter — because nobody was paying attention anymore. Epic’s challenge is not just making AI that is right. It is making AI whose alerts doctors will actually read and act on.

The Deskilling Problem: What Happens When Doctors Depend on AI?

There is a risk that almost nobody was talking about when AI first arrived in healthcare, but that has become a serious concern by 2026: deskilling. The worry is straightforward — if doctors use AI to help them do their job every day, what happens to their ability to do that job without AI?

In 2025, a landmark study published in The Lancet provided the first real evidence. Researchers studied doctors performing colonoscopies (a procedure where a camera is used to look for precancerous growths in the colon) at four hospitals in Poland. Half the time, the doctors used an AI tool that highlighted suspicious spots on the screen. The other half, they worked on their own. Before the AI was introduced, doctors found precancerous growths in about 28% of procedures. After three months of using the AI, their detection rate without AI assistance dropped to 22% — a significant decline. The AI had made them better when it was on, but worse when it was off.

The deskilling dilemma
The colonoscopy study is the first hard evidence of a pattern that researchers and doctors have been worried about for years: when AI does part of your job for you, you may gradually lose the ability to do that part yourself. This is not unique to medicine — think about how relying on GPS affects your ability to navigate without it, or how spell-check affects your ability to spell. But in healthcare, the stakes are different. A doctor who cannot detect cancer without AI assistance is a doctor who is effectively dependent on a technology that might go offline, might not be available at every hospital, or might make mistakes that the doctor can no longer catch. As one physician put it in a survey: “Medicine today has already taken a path of more reliance on the computer than the patient.” The question is whether AI reverses that trend — or accelerates it.

This tension is at the heart of clinician resistance to AI. A 2025 survey found that only 16% of physicians currently use AI tools for clinical decisions, and only 17% believe AI can make meaningful clinical suggestions. Nearly half (46%) see AI’s value as purely administrative — a paperwork tool, not a clinical partner. Many doctors welcome AI that takes away documentation burden, but resist AI that inserts itself into clinical judgment. The distinction matters: doctors are not anti-AI. They are protective of the expertise that defines their profession, and wary of becoming dependent on tools they do not fully understand or control.

Key concept
Deskilling
Deskilling is the gradual loss of professional skills that happens when people rely on technology to do work they used to do themselves. In healthcare, this can mean doctors losing their ability to spot problems without AI assistance, or medical students never fully developing diagnostic skills because AI fills in the gaps during their training. Deskilling is not the same as AI being bad — in fact, it often happens precisely because AI is good. The better the tool, the more you rely on it, and the more your own skills fade from lack of practice. The challenge for healthcare AI is designing systems that augment human skills without replacing them — keeping the human sharp even as the AI takes on more responsibility.

14 Beyond Today: What Comes Next in Healthcare AI

The agentic AI Epic is deploying in 2024 and 2025 is closer to the beginning of a long arc than to its endpoint. Here is where the evidence and the trajectory of investment suggest healthcare AI is heading.

Near term (2025–2027): Greater autonomy, task by task

The current generation of Epic’s agents operates in human-in-the-loop mode for clinical decisions. As confidence builds through validation studies and outcome data, lower-stakes tasks will shift to human-on-the-loop operation. Insurance appeal letters may be submitted automatically, with humans reviewing exceptions. Routine preventive outreach may become fully automated. The shift will happen task by task, evidence by evidence, not as a single policy change.

Two developments in 2026 accelerate this trajectory. First, Agent Factory puts the tools for building agentic workflows directly in the hands of health systems — meaning the pace of agentic AI deployment will no longer be limited to what Epic develops centrally. A hospital that identifies a gap in its discharge planning process can now build an agent to address it, rather than waiting for Epic to prioritize that use case. Second, conversational AI for clinicians will let Art respond to questions asked during patient visits in real time — handling direct questions like “Art, how is this patient’s blood pressure trending?” and surfacing context-appropriate information based on the flow of the conversation. A Diagnosis Checker will help clinicians at the point of care by analyzing patterns in patient symptoms, history, and outcomes and suggesting differential diagnoses based on real-world patient data from Cosmos.

Near-term projection (2025–2027)
Systematic care gap closure at scale: Within two to three years, health systems using Epic are likely to have fully agentic workflows that proactively identify patients overdue for preventive screenings, draft and send personalized outreach, suggest appointment scheduling, and follow up with non-responders — all without any human initiating each individual action. For a primary care practice with 2,000 patients, this kind of systematic proactive outreach has been operationally impossible. Agentic AI will make it routine. The patients who benefit most will be those who were too busy, too intimidated, or too isolated to reach out themselves.

Medium term (2027–2032): Multimodal AI and continuous monitoring

Epic’s leadership has explicitly described a roadmap toward native multimodal AI capabilities — processing not just text but video, images, and genomic data. This opens the door to AI-assisted diagnostics that go beyond pattern-matching in text records. An AI agent that can analyze a dermatology photograph, a radiology scan, and the patient’s full clinical history simultaneously — and surface a differential diagnosis for a physician to review — is within technical reach. Curiosity’s foundation models, trained on 300 million patient trajectories, will provide the predictive backbone: what typically happens next for patients with this combination of findings, and what interventions have historically made a difference.

Longer term (2032+): AI as a structural participant in care delivery

The U.S. faces a projected shortage of 86,000 physicians by 2036. AI cannot fill that gap by being a better search engine. But AI agents that handle routine chronic disease monitoring, medication refill management, follow-up on stable conditions, and patient question triage — while escalating anything requiring physician judgment — could meaningfully extend the productive capacity of each physician. This is not a future in which AI replaces doctors. It is a future in which AI makes it possible to extend physician-level oversight to populations who currently have very limited access to it.

Research is already backing up this approach. A 2025 study from Mount Sinai hospital found that splitting clinical tasks among multiple specialized AI agents — each handling one area, managed by a central system — was up to 65 times more efficient and much more accurate under heavy workloads than using a single, do-everything AI. This supports Epic’s decision to build separate specialized agents (Art for doctors, Penny for billing, Emmie for patients) rather than one giant medical AI that tries to do everything. The future is not one super-intelligent medical AI. It is a team of specialized AI agents, each great at its specific job, working together with human oversight.

Longer-term projection (2032+)
The AI primary care layer: In underserved communities, rural areas, and developing nations, physician access is already deeply constrained. AI agents trained on Cosmos-scale data and validated against millions of clinical outcomes could extend a meaningful layer of evidence-based care to populations that currently lack access to any specialist and see a primary care physician rarely if ever. This is healthcare AI’s most ambitious potential contribution — not making existing care more efficient, but making care accessible where it currently does not exist.
On the horizon — world models in medicine
From predicting the next event to simulating entire patient futures: Curiosity predicts the next likely medical event for a patient. But researchers are already working on something more ambitious: world models for healthcare — AI systems that build an internal simulation of how a patient’s body works and can “imagine” how different treatments would play out before a doctor ever tries them. Think of it like a flight simulator, but for medicine: instead of crashing a real plane to learn what happens, you simulate thousands of scenarios and pick the one that works best. A 2025 paper presented at NeurIPS (a major AI research conference) proposed exactly this — world models that combine the predictive power of systems like Curiosity with the ability to simulate “what if?” scenarios: What if we start this medication now? What if we wait three months? What if we try a different dose? Epic’s Cosmos data, with its 300 million patient trajectories, is precisely the kind of dataset these world models would need to learn from. The technology is not ready for clinical use today, but the direction is clear: the future of medical AI is not just predicting what will happen, but simulating what could happen under different choices.
The risks that accompany the progress
Every step toward giving AI more independence in healthcare comes with real risks. AI bias: if the AI learned from data that underrepresents certain groups of people, it may make worse predictions for those groups. Errors at scale: a single bug in an algorithm running at 3,600 hospitals could affect millions of patients before anyone catches it. Regulations lagging behind: the FDA (the government agency that approves medical devices) is still figuring out how to regulate AI agents. Who is responsible?: when an AI agent causes harm, who gets blamed — the hospital, Epic, Microsoft, or the doctor who trusted the AI’s output? These are not hypothetical questions. They are active legal and policy debates happening right now.

15 Key Vocabulary & Discussion Questions

Key vocabulary introduced in this chapter

Agentic AI
AI given a goal that plans and carries out multi-step processes on its own — fundamentally different from AI that responds to a single question with a single answer
Ambient AI
AI that operates passively in the background, capturing and processing information without being asked — DAX Copilot listens to clinical conversations without the physician typing a word
Electronic Health Record (EHR)
A digital system for storing and managing patient medical information across doctors, hospitals, and settings — the platform on which all healthcare AI is being built
HITECH Act
The 2009 U.S. law that paid hospitals to go digital and penalized those that did not — the policy decision that created the data foundation for everything in this chapter
Longitudinal clinical data
Patient data captured over time across multiple care settings — what teaches AI how diseases progress and what treatments work, rather than snapshots of isolated moments
Cosmos
Epic’s anonymized clinical data platform: 300+ million patients, 16+ billion data points — the data advantage that makes Epic’s AI strategy nearly impossible for competitors to replicate
Curiosity
Epic’s family of AI models trained directly on real patient data from Cosmos — predicts what comes next in a patient’s medical journey, representing Epic’s shift from AI customer to AI model builder
Agent Factory
Epic’s no-code platform that lets hospitals build, customize, and deploy their own AI agents — shifting healthcare AI from “use our tools” to “build your own tools on our platform”
Emmie
Epic’s patient-facing AI agent inside MyChart — handles billing questions, appointment scheduling, care plan follow-ups, and increasingly, health questions personalized to your own medical record
Human-in-the-loop
A system where a human must review and approve every AI output before it takes effect — Epic’s current standard for all clinical decisions
Human-on-the-loop
A system where AI acts on its own while humans monitor from a higher level and can step in — the direction healthcare AI is moving for lower-risk tasks as accuracy is proven
Alert fatigue
When doctors and nurses start ignoring AI alerts because they come too often or are poorly targeted — one of the biggest barriers to AI actually improving care, regardless of how accurate the AI is
Deskilling
The gradual loss of professional skills that happens when people rely on AI to do work they used to do themselves — a 2025 Lancet study showed doctors’ cancer detection rates dropped after months of AI-assisted procedures
Data gravity
The idea that once enough data piles up in one place, everything else gets pulled toward it — Epic’s decades of patient data force AI companies to build on Epic’s platform rather than around it
Data moat
A dataset so large and hard to replicate that it creates a lasting competitive advantage — Cosmos is arguably the most powerful data moat in healthcare
World model
An AI system that builds an internal simulation of how something works and can “imagine” different scenarios before they happen — the emerging frontier of medical AI, where the goal is simulating patient futures rather than just predicting the next event

Discussion questions

Six questions anchored in the themes of this chapter. These work equally well as written assignments or in-class discussion.

  1. The unintended consequence (Sections 3 & 4). The HITECH Act and the Affordable Care Act drove the digitization of American healthcare and created the data foundation that now makes AI possible — but they also created the burnout crisis that AI is now trying to solve. Is this a story of good policy with unintended consequences, or a failure to think ahead? What would you have done differently in 2009? Does your answer change knowing that without those laws, the data for today’s healthcare AI would not exist?
  2. Whose problem is AI solving? (Sections 4, 7, 9). This chapter presents AI as a solution to clinician burnout. But hospitals also benefit when physicians see more patients per day. Epic earns revenue. Insurance companies may pay more claims when Penny helps hospitals fight more denials. Is Epic reducing burnout because it is the right thing to do — or because it is a smart business strategy that happens to benefit doctors? Can it be both? Does the motivation matter if the outcome is the same?
  3. The gatekeeper question (Section 10). Epic controls the software, the data (Cosmos), and now its own AI models (Curiosity). It decides which third-party AI tools get access to patient data through App Orchard. Is this level of control good for patients because it maintains quality and safety standards, or is it bad for patients because it limits competition and innovation? Compare Epic’s position to Apple’s control of the App Store. What are the similarities? What is different when the product is healthcare instead of phones?
  4. The deskilling dilemma (Section 13). The Lancet colonoscopy study showed that doctors’ ability to detect cancer dropped after they got used to having AI help. If AI makes doctors better with AI but worse without it, is that a net positive or a net negative for patients? What if the AI is always available — does the deskilling risk still matter? Design a rule or practice that a hospital could adopt to prevent deskilling while still getting the benefits of AI assistance.
  5. The liability question (Section 14). An AI agent drafts a clinical note containing a dosage error. The physician reviews it, does not catch the mistake, and approves it. The patient is harmed. Who is responsible — the physician, the hospital, Epic, or Microsoft? Now change the scenario: the physician was reviewing 40 AI-generated notes that morning and had four minutes per note. Does that change your answer? Should it change how AI-generated clinical content is governed?
  6. Your career in the AI healthcare era (Section 14). The U.S. faces a shortage of 86,000 physicians by 2036, and AI agents will increasingly extend what each doctor can do. Pick a healthcare-adjacent career that interests you — hospital administration, health insurance, pharmaceuticals, public health, consulting, or health IT. Identify one specific task in that field where an AI agent is likely to be used within five years. Describe what the agent does, where the human checkpoint sits, and what skills would make you the person who works effectively with that agent — rather than the person whose job the agent replaced.
