Explore the Data
AI Factory stage: Data — see trend and seasonality in raw trip history

Before anything else, let's be clear about what forecasting actually means — because it is the foundation of everything in this lab.

Forecasting means using what happened in the past to predict what will happen next. For Uber in Bellingham, that means looking at years of trip data — every ride taken, what day it was, what time, what the weather was, whether there was an event nearby — and finding the patterns that repeat. Once you know the patterns, you can predict: next Friday at 10pm, after the bars close, in the rain, we will probably need about 340 drivers in downtown Bellingham. That prediction is what sets the surge multiplier a rider sees, and what sends an incentive notification to a driver's phone.

Without forecasting, Uber cannot set a price, cannot move drivers, cannot provision servers. The forecast is not a feature of Uber's product — it is the product. That is the central argument of the case, and it is what you are going to build from scratch in this lab.

All forecasting starts with the same question: what has happened before? Uber's forecasting system is trained on years of trip history — every ride, every city, every hour of the day. Before you can build a model, you need to understand what patterns live in that data. Two almost always appear: trend (the long-run direction, like Bellingham's trip volume growing each year as Uber expands) and seasonality (cycles that repeat on a schedule, like summers being busier than winters, or Friday nights always spiking).

Build a Forecast
AI Factory stage: Model — backtest, validate, quantify uncertainty

A forecast is not a guess. It is a structured, disciplined estimate built from historical patterns, with an honest measure of how uncertain that estimate is. Uber's forecasting team uses many model types depending on the situation — but every model does the same thing: it learns from the past to anticipate the future.

In this step you will build a simple but honest forecasting model. You are not aiming for maximum accuracy. You are aiming to understand what a forecast actually does — and crucially, to see the difference between a point forecast (one number) and a prediction interval (a range that acknowledges uncertainty).

Add plain-English comments throughout. No ARIMA, no LSTM — linear regression only.
What this prompt is actually doing
The prompt asks Claude to build your model in three phases that mirror exactly what Uber's engineering team does before deploying any model. Phase A is feature engineering — turning your raw week/trips table into something a model can actually learn from by adding three signal columns (week number, summer flag, NYE flag). Phase B is the backtest: you hide the last 26 weeks from the model, train it only on weeks 1–78, then let it predict weeks 79–104 and compare those predictions against what "actually happened." This honest test on data the model never saw is called backtesting — if it fails here, it fails in production. Phase C makes the uncertainty visible: instead of just a single predicted line, the chart shows a shaded band of ±15% around each prediction. That band is your prediction interval — the honest range of outcomes the model considers plausible. When you look at your chart, ask yourself: which weeks does the model miss badly? And what does the width of the shaded band tell an operations manager about how much buffer to build into driver recruitment?

The Algorithm Decides
AI Factory stage: Prediction → Decision — the prediction-decision gap lands here

In Step 2, your forecasting model produced a number — a prediction of how many Uber trips Bellingham will need. That number does not sit in a spreadsheet waiting for a human to review it. It goes directly into Uber's pricing system, which reads it and calculates the surge multiplier — the number a rider sees on their app that tells them what a trip will cost right now.

The surge multiplier is not set by a person. It is the direct output of the forecast you just built. When demand is predicted to be high and drivers are predicted to be scarce, the multiplier goes up automatically, in milliseconds, with no one deciding whether that is the right call in this specific moment. That is what "closing the prediction-decision gap" looks like in practice. And that is exactly where things get complicated.

From the case — Chapter 4, Sections 3 and 10
Section 3 is where the prediction-decision gap is defined and why Uber chose to close it — re-read that section now if you want the full argument before you use the simulator. Section 10 shows the four specific algorithmic decisions Uber automates: driver repositioning, surge pricing, infrastructure provisioning, and — in the Upfront Fares mini-case — driver pay itself. What you are about to simulate is surge pricing.

Break the Model
AI Factory stage: Value — distribution shift, model decay, and monitoring

The forecasting model you built in Step 2 is now deployed — meaning it is live, running in the background, producing the predictions that set the surge multiplier drivers and riders see. It passed its backtest. It looked reliable. But that backtest only proved it understood the past. The moment something changes in the real world, it is flying blind — and nobody has told it anything is different.

This is called distribution shift — and it is the main reason AI systems at companies like Uber need constant monitoring, not just a one-time test before launch. Use the simulator below to feel it happen.

From the case — Chapter 4, Section 9
Section 9 defines distribution shift using Uber's own COVID example — years of commute patterns became useless almost overnight. Read that section, then come back and use the simulator with that story in mind.

Distribution shift is a governance problem, not just a technical one
The technical fix is simple: retrain the model. The governance fix — who monitors it, how often, who has authority to pause it, who is accountable when it runs wrong for three weeks undetected — is where most companies fail. Notice that the simulator's monitoring alert is just a threshold check. Someone had to decide what that threshold should be, build the check, and make sure a human reviews it when it fires. Each of those decisions is a governance choice, not a code choice.
