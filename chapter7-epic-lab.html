The setup

You are a healthcare systems analyst at Swedish Medical Center, part of the Providence health system in Seattle. You majored in MIS and took an AI in Business class. Your manager, the VP of Revenue Cycle, pulls you aside after a meeting:

“Hey — you took that AI class, right? Have you heard of agentic AI? We have a real problem. Insurance companies deny about 17% of our claims,1 and it’s getting worse — denials are up 16% since 2018.2 Here’s the thing though: when people actually appeal, 57% of denials get overturned.1 More than half! But almost nobody appeals — fewer than 1% of patients challenge their denials.3 It’s not because the denials are right. It’s because nobody has the time. Each appeal takes our team 45 minutes to research and write.4 We just can’t fight them all. I want to know if we can build an AI agent that drafts these appeals for us — so we can fight on behalf of more patients. Can you build me a proof-of-concept?”

Your deliverable: A working prototype of an AI agent that reads an insurance denial, pulls evidence from the patient’s medical chart, and drafts an appeal letter for human review before submission.

Step 1
Research: What is agentic AI and how is it different?

Before you build anything, you need to understand what you’re building. Your manager said “figure out what agentic AI is” — so let’s start with a quick experiment that shows why the AI tools you already know how to use aren’t enough for this problem.

Try it

Open ChatGPT and Claude. Type the same prompt into each:

Write an insurance appeal letter for a denied claim for a knee MRI.

ChatGPT may not even understand the domain — it might ask if this is about a parking ticket or academic dismissal. Claude understands it is healthcare and asks the right follow-up questions. But both are asking you to do the legwork. An agent would go get the information itself.

Three levels of AI (terminology still evolving)

Level 1 — LLMs: Text in, text out. Cannot look anything up or take action.

Level 2 — AI Agents (tools, but you direct each step): You say “Create a Google Sheet for workshop scheduling with columns for date, time, speaker, and room.” It connects to Sheets and builds it — but you planned every detail.

Level 3 — Agentic AI (you design a workflow, then give it a goal): You build a workflow: connect the agent to a patient chart tool and a policy lookup tool, write a system prompt defining its role, and set guardrails. Then you say “Fight this insurance denial.” The AI decides within that workflow to read the denial, pull the chart, check the policy, and draft the appeal. You designed the system. The AI planned the steps.

Level 2 vs. 3: Who decides the steps? At Level 2, you prompt each action. At Level 3, you describe the goal and the AI figures out how.

But agentic AI needs a workflow. Someone has to define the goal, choose the tools, set guardrails, and decide where a human approves. The AI runs the loop. A human designs it. That is an IS job.

Emerging role — Agentic Architect

As companies move from experimenting with AI to deploying agentic workflows in production, a new role is taking shape: the Agentic Architect. This person doesn’t write the AI models — they design the systems around them. They decide what tools an agent can access, where humans stay in the loop, what governance guardrails to enforce, and how to connect agents to real business processes. Think of it as the difference between building an engine and designing the car around it. Organizations need people who understand both the technology and the business well enough to make these design decisions. If you can define an agent’s scope, choose its tools, write its system prompt, and design its oversight model — that is exactly what an Agentic Architect does. It’s what you are practicing in this lab.

Step 3
Break the AI insurance advocate

Nice — you have a working agent. It read the denial, pulled the chart, checked the policy, and drafted a real appeal letter. But before you show this to your manager, you need to answer the question she’s definitely going to ask: “What happens when something goes wrong?”

A good analyst doesn’t just show that something works. They show where it breaks, and whether it fails safely. Let’s find out.

Deactivate the Patient Chart tool (right-click the node → Deactivate). Run the same denial letter again.

The AI Insurance Advocate will not fabricate details — because we told it not to. Instead it falls back to asking you for the information, just like Claude did in Step 1. Without tools, the AI Insurance Advocate drops from Level 3 to Level 2.

Two lessons

1. The system prompt matters. We told it not to hallucinate, and it listened. Without that guardrail, it might have fabricated clinical evidence that looks professional but is wrong.

2. Tools make an agent an agent. Same AI, same brain. With tools = Level 3. Without = Level 2. The intelligence didn’t change. The access did.

Reactivate the Patient Chart before continuing.

Step 5
Audit the output — is the AI Insurance Advocate actually right?

At this point you’ve built the agent, broken it, and tested it on two completely different patients. The appeal letters look professional, the agent calls the right tools, and the whole thing runs in under a minute. Your manager is ready to take this to leadership — but she has one more question: “How do we know the output is actually accurate?”

This is where the real work starts. Generative AI is really good at producing things that look right — professional formatting, confident tone, specific-sounding details. But looking right and being right are not the same thing. Your job as an IS professional isn’t just to build the system. It’s to understand how it works well enough to know when it’s wrong, explain why, and fix it.

Line-by-line audit

Go back to the Maria Santos appeal letter the AI Insurance Advocate produced in Step 2. Read it line by line and check every factual claim against the source documents:

  • Does every date in the letter match the patient chart?
  • Does every clinical finding (test results, pain scores, exam results) match what the chart actually says?
  • Does every policy reference match the actual policy language in the BlueCross HIP-4420 document?
  • Did the AI Insurance Advocate add anything that is not in the source documents? If so, that is a hallucination — even if it sounds plausible.
  • Did it miss anything important that is in the chart but was left out of the letter?
Why this matters for your career

Anyone can paste a prompt into an AI tool and get something that looks impressive. The value of an IS professional is understanding the system design underneath — what data the AI used, where it came from, how it was processed, and whether the output can be trusted. When your manager asks “how does this work?” she does not want to hear “I typed a prompt and it gave me this.” She wants to hear “the agent pulled data from these two sources, cross-referenced the denial reason against the policy criteria, and constructed the argument from specific chart entries — and I verified each claim against the source.” That is the difference between using AI and understanding AI.

Step 6
AI governance — evaluate the agent you just built

So far you’ve built the agent, broken it on purpose, tested it on a second patient, and audited its output line by line. You know it works, you know where it struggles, and you can verify its accuracy. Now your manager has the big-picture question: “If we actually deployed this, what governance would we need around it?”

This is the question that separates a prototype from a production system. Building an AI agent is the easy part. Governing it — deciding what it’s allowed to do, what data it can touch, where humans must intervene, how you prove it works, and what triggers a shutdown — is what makes it safe enough to use on real patients.

Five layers of AI governance

Every AI agent — whether it is Epic’s Penny handling thousands of insurance appeals or the prototype you just built — needs governance across five layers. These are the five questions you must answer before any agent goes live:

1. Purpose & scope: What is this agent allowed to do? What is explicitly out of bounds?

2. Data access & privacy: What data can the agent access? Under what rules? How is sensitive information protected?

3. Human oversight design: Where are the human checkpoints? Which outputs require human approval before taking effect?

4. Testing, auditing & accountability: How do you prove the agent works? Who reviews its outputs? Who is responsible when it makes a mistake?

5. Monitoring & sunset: How do you track performance over time? What triggers a review or shutdown?

Apply the framework to the AI Insurance Advocate

Walk through each layer for the agent you just built. Be specific — you have already seen this agent work, break, and make mistakes (or not). Use what you learned in Steps 2–5 to answer these:

1. Purpose & scope. The AI Insurance Advocate drafts appeal letters for denied insurance claims. But what should it never do? Should it be allowed to submit appeals without human review? Should it contact patients directly? Should it make clinical recommendations? Write two or three sentences defining the boundaries — what is in scope and what is explicitly off-limits.

2. Data access & privacy. Your agent accesses patient medical charts and insurance policy documents. In a real hospital, those charts contain deeply sensitive information protected by federal law (HIPAA). What data does the agent actually need to draft an appeal letter? Is there anything in a patient’s chart the agent should not be allowed to see? How would you ensure the agent does not store or leak patient data?

3. Human oversight design. Right now, the agent drafts a letter and a human reviews it before submission. But imagine your hospital processes 500 denials per month. Is it realistic for a human to carefully review every single letter? If not, what rules would you set? Would you let some categories of appeals go out with less review? Which ones, and why?

4. Testing, auditing & accountability. You audited the Maria Santos letter in Step 5. That is one letter. How would you audit the system at scale? What metrics would you track (accuracy rate, error types, appeal success rate)? If the agent produces a letter with a factual error and it gets submitted, who is accountable — the agent, the reviewer, the hospital, or the vendor?

5. Monitoring & sunset. Insurance companies change their policies. Medical guidelines evolve. New denial patterns emerge. How would you know if the agent’s performance is degrading over time? What specific signal would tell you the agent needs to be retrained, reconfigured, or shut down?

Why this matters beyond healthcare

This governance framework applies to any agentic AI, not just healthcare. A customer service agent needs defined scope (what complaints can it resolve on its own?), data rules (can it access payment information?), human checkpoints (when does it escalate to a person?), auditing (how do you catch bias in its responses?), and monitoring (is customer satisfaction improving or declining?). The domain changes. The governance questions do not. If you can govern an AI agent in healthcare — the highest-stakes environment there is — you can govern one anywhere.

Step 7
Should we be using AI agents for this?

You’ve built it, tested it, audited it, and written the governance framework. The prototype works and you know how to make it safe. But your manager doesn’t just want a demo — she wants your honest recommendation. She asks: “What should I tell leadership about the risks?”

Before you say “yes, let’s deploy this,” step back and think through the harder questions that nobody asks during the demo:

  • AI arms race: Hospitals use AI to fight denials; insurers use AI to make denials. Does the overarching healthcare system improve for patients, or just get faster at being broken?
  • Jobs: 15-person appeals team becomes 5 people reviewing AI output. Augmentation or replacement?
  • Transparency: Should patients or insurers know the appeal was AI-written?
  • Errors at scale: 2% error rate × 1,000 monthly denials = 20 patients with wrong information.
  • Equity: Large systems can afford this. Rural clinics cannot. Does the gap widen?
