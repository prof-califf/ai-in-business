Airbnb was founded in 2008 by Brian Chesky, Joe Gebbia, and Nathan Blecharczyk. The timing was not a coincidence. The original pitch was absurdly simple — rent out an air mattress in your apartment during a design conference when hotels were sold out. But the company launched into the teeth of the 2008 financial crisis, and that context shaped everything about the business. Millions of Americans were losing their jobs, their homes, or both. The idea that you could make rent by letting a stranger sleep in your spare room went from eccentric to urgent almost overnight. Early Airbnb hosts were not lifestyle entrepreneurs. Many of them were people trying to hold onto their apartments. The platform grew because the crisis made sharing space an economic necessity — and that origin story still matters, because the same economic dynamics that created Airbnb are now driving the backlash against it.

That idea grew into one of the largest travel platforms in the world, with more than 8 million active listings in over 220 countries and regions, and more than 5 million hosts who earn income by renting space to travelers.

What makes Airbnb different from a hotel chain, an airline, or even a platform like Booking.com is the shape of the business. Airbnb owns no properties. It does not employ the people who clean the rooms, answer the doors, or set the prices. Every one of its listings is a unique home offered by an independent host, and every booking is a transaction between two people who may never meet. Airbnb’s job is to be the layer in the middle that makes those transactions happen.

That middle-layer position turns out to be an enormous AI problem. Every night, millions of potential guests are trying to figure out where to go, and millions of hosts are trying to figure out what to charge. Neither side fully knows what they want, and neither side can be expected to. The guest has a vague idea of a trip; the host has a vague sense of what similar homes nearby are charging. Airbnb’s job is to turn those two vague signals into a specific match at a specific price on a specific date — hundreds of thousands of times a day.

Strategic context
Netflix uses AI to pick the best image for a show. Spotify uses AI to pick the next song. Uber uses AI to forecast demand. Airbnb has to do all of those kinds of things — but on two sides of a marketplace at the same time. That is the defining challenge of the business, and it is the lens through which every AI decision in this chapter should be read.

2 The Big Idea: Two Sides, One Platform

Every business in this course so far has had one primary customer. Netflix has subscribers. Spotify has listeners. Uber has riders (yes, drivers matter, but the business problem is framed around getting a rider a ride). Airbnb is structurally different. Airbnb has two customers who need completely different things from the same platform — and the platform only works when both sides get what they need.

Key concept
Two-sided marketplace and the matching problem
A two-sided marketplace is a platform that brings together two user groups who need each other — buyers and sellers, riders and drivers, guests and hosts. The platform’s core job is matching: connecting a person on one side to the right person on the other, at the right moment, at a price both accept. If either side has a bad experience, the other side suffers. Every AI system Airbnb builds is ultimately in service of better matching.

Think about what that means in practice. A hotel chain like Marriott has one customer: the traveler. Marriott owns its own rooms, sets its own prices, and controls the experience end to end. If demand drops, Marriott lowers prices. If a guest wants a room, Marriott has one ready. The business is one-sided. Airbnb owns nothing. Every listing belongs to someone else, and that someone else has their own goals, their own calendar, and their own idea of what their home is worth. Airbnb has to make both sides happy simultaneously — and if either side has a bad experience, the other side suffers too. A guest who can’t find a good listing leaves. A host whose calendar stays empty stops hosting. The platform dies from either direction.

Now here is the twist that shapes the rest of the chapter: neither side of the Airbnb marketplace fully knows what it wants. That might sound strange — aren’t people who visit a travel website the ones with an idea of where they want to go? Sometimes. But often, no.

Airbnb’s users fall into two categories that require completely different experiences. Transactional users know what they want — they’re searching for a specific city on specific dates because their trip is already planned. Exploratory users don’t — they’re thinking “I want to go somewhere in France this summer” or just “I need a vacation.” Both types open the same app and click the same search bar, but the right experience for each is completely different. A good recommendation for a transactional user is a listing; a good recommendation for an exploratory user is a destination. A business that can’t tell these users apart will frustrate both of them.

If you have ever opened a travel app and just scrolled — no dates in mind, no city in mind, just a vague itch to go somewhere — you were an exploratory user. And you were giving the platform almost nothing to work with. No search query. No dates. Maybe a few past bookings from a year ago. The system needs to figure out what to show you, and it has to do it in the few seconds before you get bored and close the app.

Exploratory users introduce an even harder problem for any recommendation system: if the user doesn’t know where they want to go, how is the system supposed to know what to suggest? This is a classic problem in recommender systems, and it shows up whenever a user has very little history for the system to learn from.

Key concept
The cold-start problem
The cold-start problem is what happens when a recommendation system has to personalize for someone it knows almost nothing about. A new user has no bookings, no viewing history — standard “people like you” logic has nothing to work with. At Airbnb, cold-start includes a subtler variant: a user who booked once a year ago but hasn’t returned since. On the supply side, the same problem appears: a brand-new listing has no booking history, which is why Smart Pricing relies on patterns from similar listings.

On the host side, the parallel is nearly exact. A host has a home, a calendar, and an approximate sense of what to charge. But they don’t know what a Thursday in early October will actually be worth three months from now — nobody does. Markets move. Big events pull demand forward. Weather shifts bookings around. A host trying to price by intuition is guessing against information the platform already has in aggregate. That is the supply-side version of the same fundamental problem: the person on this side of the marketplace does not fully know what they want (or what they should charge), and the platform is better positioned to help them figure it out than they are on their own.

Guests need help discovering destinations. Hosts need help pricing listings. Both problems are really the same problem — understanding demand patterns at scale — viewed from opposite ends.

Part I — Guest Side4 Data: The Language of Travel Intent

Here is a problem. A user opens the Airbnb app. They have done four things in the last three months: booked a home in Lisbon, searched twice for “Tokyo” without booking, clicked on a listing in Mexico City, and opened the app today without typing anything. What does that tell you about where they want to go next?

A human looking at that list can probably spot a pattern — an adventurous traveler who likes cities, maybe thinking about somewhere warm. But a machine cannot reason that way unless the data is structured to let it. Before this model existed, Airbnb’s search experience was essentially reactive: you typed a city, you got listings. If you didn’t type anything, the app had nothing to show you. That worked fine for the transactional user who already had a trip planned. It was useless for the exploratory user who was still figuring out where to go — and Airbnb’s own data showed that a large share of users who opened the app fell into that exploratory category. They were the hardest users to serve and, potentially, the most valuable to convert.

The central insight in Airbnb’s destination recommendation system is that a user’s actions can be treated the way a language model treats words. To understand what that means, you need one piece of vocabulary: a token is the smallest unit of input that a model processes. In a language model like ChatGPT or Claude, each word (or piece of a word) is a token. The model reads a sequence of tokens and learns to predict which token comes next. “Peanut butter and” is three tokens, and the model has learned that the next one is probably “jelly.” The key move Airbnb makes is treating user actions as tokens — not words, but clicks, searches, and bookings — and feeding them into the same kind of model.

Each action a user takes — a booking in Lisbon, a search for Tokyo, a click on a Mexico City listing — is treated as a token in the user’s personal travel history, and sequences of those tokens become training data. The model learns that certain patterns of past actions tend to lead to certain future destinations, in the same way a language model learns that certain sequences of words tend to lead to certain next words. This is a powerful reframe: it means Airbnb can reuse the architecture of modern language models (transformers) to understand travel intent — a problem that has nothing to do with language on its face.

That reframe is worth sitting with, because it is not obvious. Travel behavior does not look like language. Nobody “reads” a sequence of Airbnb bookings the way they read a sentence. But the mathematical structure turns out to be surprisingly similar: in both cases, you have a sequence of events in order, and the goal is to predict what comes next based on what came before. Once Airbnb’s data scientists saw that parallel, they could borrow a powerful type of AI model called a transformer — the same technology behind ChatGPT and Claude — that researchers had already spent years building for language. They did not have to invent a new approach to travel recommendation. They repurposed one that already worked for a different problem.

But not every action carries the same meaning. A booking three years ago and a search three minutes ago are both data points, but they say very different things about what a user wants right now. Airbnb’s system is explicitly designed to pull two different kinds of signal out of the same behavioral sequence.

Not every action carries the same meaning, and the model is designed to distinguish two kinds of signal. Short-term signals — views and searches in the last few days — reveal what the user is actively considering right now. Long-term signals — historical bookings — reveal durable preferences: this person likes coastal cities, books in summer, tends toward mid-range prices. If Airbnb only looks at recent searches, it overreacts to momentary curiosity. If it only looks at history, it ignores that the user is actively researching Tokyo right now. The model explicitly combines both, which is why it looks at booking history, view history, and search history as separate inputs rather than one giant list.

On top of these behavioral sequences, Airbnb layers contextual signals — things like what time of year it is. A user’s long-term pattern might say “beach destinations,” but if they’re searching in December, the model should probably weight warm-weather beaches over Cape Cod. The current date is an input to the model in the same way a word’s position in a sentence is an input to a language model.

5 Model: Embeddings, Transformers, and Region/City Prediction

Once you’ve decided to treat user actions as tokens, you need a way to represent each token as something a model can actually do math with. Spotify’s chapter introduced the answer: embeddings.

Key concept
Embeddings (at Airbnb)
Recall from Chapter 3: an embedding is a compact list of numbers that represents everything the system has learned about an item. Similar items get similar embeddings and end up close together in a mathematical space; very different items end up far apart. Spotify uses embeddings for songs and users. Airbnb uses them for cities, regions, and dates. Lisbon gets an embedding, Porto gets an embedding, and if they co-appear in enough guests’ travel patterns, the two embeddings end up close together — meaning someone who shows interest in one is likely to be interested in the other. Each user action in the sequence gets its own embedding that combines three pieces of information: the city, the region that city belongs to, and how long ago it happened. That single compact representation captures both what the user did and when.

Once each action is an embedding, the model processes the whole sequence of actions with a transformer — the same class of architecture that powers modern language models like the ones behind ChatGPT and Claude. The technical details are beyond the scope of this course, but the business intuition is straightforward: a transformer is very good at looking at a sequence and figuring out which parts of it matter most for predicting what comes next. When the user’s history is “booked Lisbon two years ago, searched Tokyo three times last week, clicked Mexico City yesterday,” the transformer learns which of those actions deserves the most weight in predicting today’s destination.

But there is one more wrinkle. The model is not just trying to predict a city. It is trying to predict geography at multiple levels at once.

The model uses a technique called multi-task learning: instead of predicting just one thing, it predicts two related things at once — both which region a user is interested in (say, the San Francisco Bay Area) and which specific city within that region (San Francisco, Oakland, San Jose). The two predictions are forced to be consistent with each other. This matters because users who like San Francisco often end up booking in San Jose or Oakland, and training on both tasks at once gives the model more signal to learn from than training on either alone.

The diagram below shows how this works. The “shared trunk” in the middle is the core of the model — the transformer that processes the user’s action sequence and builds up an understanding of their travel intent. Think of it as the part of the model that does the heavy thinking. That shared understanding then feeds into two separate “heads” at the top — one that predicts the region and one that predicts the city. The trunk is shared because the same patterns that help the model understand regions (this user likes coastal Europe) also help it understand cities (they might like Lisbon specifically). By sharing the trunk instead of building two separate models, Airbnb gets richer predictions from the same data.

6 Prediction, Decision, and Value: From Dormant Users to Autosuggest

Here is a training-data puzzle worth sitting with. Airbnb wants the destination model to work for everyone — not just the user who just searched five times in the last week, but also the user who booked once in 2024 and hasn’t logged in since. If you train the model only on users who are currently active, you get a model that is great at the obvious case and useless for the harder, more valuable case of bringing back a lapsed user. If you train it only on dormant users, you can’t serve the active ones well. Airbnb’s answer is to design the training data to include both.

Airbnb’s solution is a training-data design choice. Every past booking generates not one but fourteen training examples: seven that simulate an active user in the final week of planning (using recent searches and views), and seven that simulate a dormant user who hasn’t visited the platform in months (using only historical booking data, because a dormant user has no recent activity). This deliberately teaches the model to handle both situations. It is a reminder that the people designing AI systems make decisions about who the system is being built for before any code is written.

That number — fourteen training examples from a single booking — is easy to read past, but it is one of the most important design decisions in the entire system. It means Airbnb’s engineers made a deliberate choice about who this model would serve before they wrote a single line of model code. They could have trained only on active users, which would have been easier and produced better accuracy on the easy cases. Instead, they chose to build a system that would also work for the user who booked a cabin in Vermont eighteen months ago and hasn’t opened the app since. That decision has business consequences (dormant users are a huge re-engagement opportunity) and ethical ones (a model built only for power users implicitly excludes everyone else).

Once the model is trained, Airbnb has a predicted region and a predicted city for each user. But a prediction is not a decision. What should the business actually do with it?

But a prediction is not a decision — the prediction–decision gap from Chapter 2 applies here too. The model predicts “this user is interested in Tokyo, Osaka, and Kyoto,” but that prediction on its own does nothing. Airbnb has to turn it into specific actions: which three cities to show in autosuggest when the user taps the search bar, which destination to feature in an abandoned-search email, how prominently to surface each one. The destination model is the inference engine. The product decisions around it are where the business value actually lives.

Airbnb has deployed the destination model in two places so far, and the two deployments illustrate opposite ends of the user-intent spectrum:

  • Autosuggest. When a user taps the search bar, the system shows a ranked list of suggested destinations before they’ve typed anything. This is the active user case — someone who just opened the app is in a decision-making moment, and the right three suggestions can shave real time and friction off the trip-planning process. Airbnb reports that this feature drives the largest booking gains in regions where English is not the primary language — suggesting it is especially helpful for users who have a harder time typing and spelling unfamiliar destination names.
  • Abandoned-search emails. When a user searches but doesn’t book, Airbnb sends a follow-up email featuring listings in destinations the model predicts the user is most likely to want — not necessarily the exact destination they searched. This is the dormant-user case: the user has shown some interest and then left, and the email is an attempt to re-engage them with something a little broader than what they searched for.
Business insight
Notice how the same model powers two completely different product experiences. Autosuggest is a real-time, high-intent surface — the user is literally about to type. Abandoned-search email is a delayed, low-intent surface — the user has left and may or may not come back. One model, trained once on cleverly designed data, now serves both. This is the economics of AI that matures: the most expensive part of an AI system is usually not the model itself — it’s assembling the data and infrastructure. Once that’s done, the same prediction can be deployed across many surfaces at low marginal cost.

The value: Higher conversion for active users who find what they want faster, and re-engagement of dormant users who might otherwise never come back. The destination recommender does not generate revenue directly — nobody pays for a recommendation. It generates revenue by shortening the path from “I want to go somewhere” to “I just booked.” That is the AI Factory pattern: data feeds models, models produce predictions, predictions inform decisions, and decisions create business value that generates more data.

Part II — Host Side7 Data: Dynamic Pricing and Lead Time

Now flip the marketplace around. A host has just listed a two-bedroom apartment in San Diego. They need to decide what to charge for every single night on their calendar — not just tonight, but six months from now, twelve months from now. Prices that are too high leave the calendar empty. Prices that are too low leave money on the table. And the “right” price for a Thursday in early October is different in July than it is in September, because the market keeps moving.

Before Smart Pricing, most hosts did what you would probably do: they looked at a few nearby listings, picked a round number that felt reasonable, and left it there for months. Some hosts used spreadsheets. A small number of professional property managers used third-party pricing tools. But the vast majority of Airbnb’s millions of hosts are not professional hoteliers — they are people renting a spare room or a vacation home, and they do not have the time, the data, or the expertise to reprice their listing every week as the market shifts. The result was widespread mispricing: hosts in high-demand markets charging too little and booking instantly (leaving money on the table), and hosts in soft markets charging too much and sitting empty for weeks (losing income entirely).

Key concept
Dynamic pricing
Dynamic pricing is the practice of adjusting the price of a product or service as market conditions change — demand rises, supply gets tight, competitors adjust their prices. Airlines have done this for decades; hotels and ride-sharing platforms do it now; Airbnb does it through a feature called Smart Pricing, which suggests a nightly price for each date on a host’s calendar and updates those suggestions as the booking date approaches. For Airbnb hosts specifically, dynamic pricing is harder than it is for a hotel, because every listing is unique. There’s no “standard double room” to benchmark against — every home has its own size, quality, location, and personality. That uniqueness is the heart of the problem the pricing model has to solve.
Mini-case · Dynamic pricing gone wrong
Ticketmaster and the Oasis reunion (2024)

Dynamic pricing is not always received well. In August 2024, Oasis announced their first reunion tour in 16 years. Millions of fans entered Ticketmaster’s online queue. Many waited hours. When they finally reached the checkout, standing tickets that had been advertised at £148.50 were now listed at over £350. “Platinum” tickets were priced at nearly 2.5 times the standard price, with no additional benefits — same section, same view, just a fancier label.

The public fury was intense. The UK’s Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) investigated. Interestingly, the CMA found no evidence that Ticketmaster actually used algorithmic dynamic pricing — prices were not adjusting in real time based on demand. What happened was simpler: there were two preset tiers of standing tickets, and once the cheaper tier sold out, the price jumped to the higher tier. Ticketmaster had not told fans waiting in the queue that this would happen.

The CMA secured commitments from Ticketmaster to disclose tiered pricing 24 hours in advance, show the full range of prices when fans join a queue, and stop using misleading ticket labels. But the episode reveals something important about dynamic pricing: the public backlash was not about the prices themselves — it was about the lack of transparency. Fans felt trapped. They had waited hours, had no alternative (there is only one Oasis reunion), and discovered the price change only at the moment of purchase.

Compare that to Airbnb. A guest shopping for a listing in Barcelona can see the price before they commit, compare it against other listings, try different dates, or pick a different city entirely. The host — not an algorithm — makes the final pricing decision. The price adjusts over weeks and months, not minutes. Dynamic pricing feels fair when the buyer has information (what will I pay?), alternatives (can I go elsewhere?), and time (can I decide without pressure?). Ticketmaster failed on all three. Airbnb mostly passes — though a guest searching last-minute in a city with very few available listings during a major event is closer to the Ticketmaster end of the spectrum than Airbnb might like to admit.

One of the most important variables in pricing is lead time — the gap between when a booking is made and the check-in date. A guest booking New Year’s Eve on December 1st has a lead time of 30 days; a last-minute booking for tonight has a lead time of zero. The lead time distribution is the shape of how bookings spread across those gaps for a given date and location. High-demand dates like New Year’s Eve see early bookings — people plan ahead. Beach towns see more last-minute bookings. A city like San Francisco during a big conference gets booked weeks ahead because there aren’t enough listings to go around. If a host prices a peak date as if bookings will come in the last two weeks, they’ll leave the calendar open too long and miss the early wave. Understanding the lead time distribution is what lets Smart Pricing tell the host when to raise prices and when to lower them.

Bookings arrive over time in a way that looks random at any single moment but has clear patterns when you zoom out. You can’t predict exactly when the next booking for a specific listing will come in, but across thousands of listings and check-in dates, the overall pattern is highly predictable. Smart Pricing’s job is not to guess the next individual booking — it’s to forecast the shape of the arrival pattern for each check-in date, so hosts can price appropriately as the date approaches. Framed this way, dynamic pricing becomes a forecasting problem with a pricing decision bolted onto the end.

8 Model: Why Pure ML Wasn’t Enough

Here is where the Smart Pricing story gets interesting — and where it connects directly back to Uber. The obvious move for a company like Airbnb is to throw a powerful machine learning model at the problem. Collect every feature you can find about each listing and each check-in date, train a huge model to predict the optimal price, and deploy it. Airbnb’s data science team considered exactly that approach, and rejected it.

The reasons were specific. With millions of listings, each with its own characteristics, and each checkin date getting booked at most once, there simply is not enough data at the individual-listing level. The prediction problem involves three things at once — which listing, which check-in date, and how far in advance — which makes it extremely complex to compute. And perhaps most importantly, Airbnb’s team had good domain intuition about the shape of the lead time distribution — they could see that bookings tended to accumulate in a recognizable pattern — a shape that statisticians have studied for decades. A pure ML approach would ignore all of that intuition.

A structural model is one where a human expert specifies the general shape of the relationship, and the model’s job is just to fill in the parameters. A pure ML model specifies no shape — it learns everything from data. ML models tend to be more accurate with enough data; structural models tend to be more interpretable. Airbnb’s Smart Pricing team refused to pick one. Instead, they built a hybrid.

This is a decision that comes up constantly in applied AI, and it is worth understanding concretely. Imagine you are on Airbnb’s data science team and you are staring at a chart of how bookings accumulate over time for a popular beach house. You can see the shape: bookings trickle in slowly at first, then accelerate as the check-in date gets closer, then taper off in the last few days. That shape looks like a curve you might recognize from a statistics class — a pattern that has been well-studied. A pure ML model would have to discover that shape from scratch, using only the data. A structural model would assume that shape and just ask the ML to fill in the specific numbers. The question is: do you trust your domain knowledge enough to bake it in?

Key concept
Hybrid models: ML + domain knowledge
A hybrid model lets a human expert specify the shape of the relationship, and reduces the ML model’s job to predicting just the parameters that define that shape. Smart Pricing does exactly this: instead of asking ML to learn a full demand curve from scratch, it assumes bookings follow a known statistical pattern and asks the model to predict just five numbers that define the curve for each listing cluster and date. The result: forecast error was cut roughly in half compared to pure ML, and the predictions were explainable enough to show hosts.

But there’s still one problem left. Airbnb has millions of listings, and each one is unique. Even with a hybrid model, trying to calculate five numbers for every individual listing hits the same wall — most listings simply don’t have enough bookings to work with. Consider: a cozy studio apartment in Lisbon might get booked 15 times a year. That is 15 data points to learn from — not nearly enough data to learn a reliable pattern from. A rural farmhouse in Tuscany might get booked 4 times. The model needs thousands of bookings to calculate those five numbers reliably, and individual listings will never provide that. The workaround is an idea that should feel familiar from Spotify.

Airbnb’s solution uses clustering — a technique where the system groups similar items together on its own, without anyone telling it what the groups should be. (Spotify uses the same idea to group songs by audience.) By looking at which listings guests tend to consider together in a single search session, the system learns which homes share similar audiences. Listings that frequently co-appear in browsing get grouped into clusters Airbnb calls demand aggregations. Each cluster shares a lead time distribution, which solves the not-enough-data problem: instead of trying to learn from one listing with 15 bookings a year, the model learns from a cluster of thousands of listings that share a common audience.

Why this matters for managers
Pure ML is not always the best answer — even when you have lots of data and lots of compute. Smart Pricing is a case where combining a human intuition about the shape of the problem with an ML model produced better results than pure ML alone. This is a recurring pattern in applied AI. Teams that know their domain well enough to say “the answer has to take this form” often beat teams that just throw bigger models at the problem. AI does not replace expertise; it amplifies it. A manager evaluating an AI proposal should ask not just “what model?” but “what do we know about this problem that the model doesn’t need to learn from scratch?”

9 Prediction, Decision, and Value: The Host Side

Smart Pricing takes what the model has learned about booking patterns for each cluster of similar listings, combines it with details about the specific listing and the specific check-in date, and produces a suggested nightly price. That suggestion gets displayed in the host’s calendar view. The host can accept it, override it, or ignore it entirely.

That single design choice — suggest, don’t set — is worth pausing on, because it’s a live example of a tension this course has been tracking since Chapter 2.

This is a deliberate choice between automation and augmentation — the same tradeoff from Chapter 2. Airbnb could set prices directly; it has the data and the models. But it chose augmentation: the model recommends, the host decides. Why? Partly legal (Airbnb doesn’t own the home), partly ethical (the host bears the consequences of a bad price), and partly strategic (hosts who feel coerced churn off the platform). The same AI capability that could have been automation is deployed as augmentation because of a business judgment about the relationship between platform and host.

That design choice does not make the ethical questions go away. When Smart Pricing suggests lowering a nightly rate to match an apparent market softening, the host is under real pressure to accept — the alternative is a dark calendar. The suggestion is not a command, but it is not neutral either. A suggestion from an algorithm that sees the entire market is hard to argue with from a position of seeing just your own property. We’ll return to this in §11.

The business takeaway from Parts I and II
Parts I and II teach the same lesson from opposite sides of the marketplace. When neither side of a transaction can fully articulate what they want, the platform that can infer it from behavior has an enormous advantage — but that advantage comes with a responsibility, because inference at scale shapes markets in ways no individual user can see or contest. The guest-side model infers intent. The host-side model infers price. In both cases, the AI is doing something no human could do manually: reading millions of behavioral signals and converting them into a specific, actionable recommendation in real time. That is the core AI capability this chapter teaches. Everything that follows — the LLM migration, the responsible-AI questions, the competitive moat — builds on it.

Part III10 LLMs for Test Migration: 1.5 Years to 6 Weeks

The first two parts of this chapter looked at AI systems Airbnb built to serve its customers. This third part is different. It is about a system Airbnb built to serve itself — and it is the clearest example in this course so far of how generative AI is actually being integrated into companies that already have deep ML capability.

The problem: Airbnb had about 3,500 files of automated software tests — code that checks whether other code is working correctly. These tests were written using an older tool called Enzyme, which was becoming obsolete. They needed to be rewritten using a newer tool called React Testing Library. The rewritten tests had to check all the same things the old tests checked — you can’t just delete tests without losing the safety net they provide. Engineers originally estimated this rewrite at about 1.5 years of engineering time if done by hand. In 2025, Airbnb completed it using a pipeline built around a large language model in six weeks.

That is not a small result, and the underlying mechanics matter more than the headline number. To understand what Airbnb actually did, you first need a clear distinction between the kind of AI we’ve been discussing in this course and the kind of AI that handled this migration.

Key concept
Generative AI vs. predictive AI
Every AI system in this course until now has been predictive. A predictive model is trained to answer a specific question (what’s the click-through rate? what’s the demand? where does this user want to travel?) by learning patterns in historical data. The output is usually a number or a ranked list. Generative AI is different. It produces new content — text, code, images — that didn’t exist before, by learning patterns from huge collections of unstructured data. Large language models (LLMs) like the ones behind ChatGPT and Claude are generative. Airbnb’s destination recommender is predictive: it takes a user’s history and predicts a destination. An LLM is generative: it takes a prompt and produces new code. The two kinds of AI are built differently, scale differently, fail differently, and should be governed differently — but an increasing number of real production systems (including the one in this section) stitch them together.

Why was this problem a good fit for an LLM and not for the kind of custom model built in Parts I and II? Three reasons. First, the task is code, not behavior — there’s an enormous amount of public JavaScript code in the world that an LLM has already been trained on. Second, the task is generative, not predictive — the output is a new test file, not a score or a ranking. Third, the task has an objective correctness check — the migrated test either passes or fails when you run it. You don’t need a human to rate the output; the machine can tell immediately whether it worked. That third property is crucial, and it led directly to the most important design decision in the whole project.

The pipeline: the LLM is the engine; the workflow is the engineering

Here is the part that business students should pay closest attention to. Airbnb’s engineers did not just hand 3,500 files to a language model and hope for the best. They built an automated pipeline around the model that looked more like a factory than a chatbot. The structure was roughly:

  1. Break the job into small, checkable steps. Each file was pushed through a sequence of distinct stages — refactor the Enzyme code, fix any issues in the test framework, fix formatting and code-quality errors, then mark complete. Each stage had a validation check: did the output actually work?
  2. When a step fails, retry with better context. If a stage failed, the system automatically re-prompted the LLM — this time including the specific error message and the most recent broken version of the file. (Engineers call this dynamic prompting: the prompt changes on each retry based on what went wrong, rather than sending the same instructions every time.) Most files that failed the first attempt succeeded within about 10 retries, though the hardest files were retried 50 to 100 times before the team gave up on automation.
  3. For the hard cases, give the model more to work with. On complex files, the prompts grew to include up to 50 related files from the same project — sibling test files written in the right style, the source code of the component being tested, examples of well-written tests. Prompts grew to the equivalent of 30,000–75,000 words each — roughly the length of a novel.
  4. Run the loop at industrial scale. Files ran in parallel, progress was tracked by automatically inserting a comment into each file recording its migration status, and engineers could re-run any specific step against any specific subset of files. After the first bulk run, 75% of files were done in four hours.
  5. Chip away at the long tail. For the remaining 25%, the team ran what they called a “sample, tune, sweep” loop: look at a handful of stuck files, figure out the common issue, update the prompts to fix it, re-run everything. Four days of this got them to 97%. The last 3% were fixed by hand. Notably, the team found that the biggest gains came not from cleverer prompt wording, but from choosing the right related files to include as context — sibling tests, the component source code, examples of well-written tests from the same project. The bottleneck was curation of inputs, not cleverness of instructions.

The most important lesson from the migration is not about the LLM — it’s about everything around the LLM. The language model is one component in a much larger system: a validation pipeline, a retry loop, a context-gathering mechanism, and a human-directed tuning loop. The LLM provides capability; the engineering around it provides reliability. You could swap in a different LLM tomorrow and the system would keep working. Remove the validation and retry machinery, and the raw LLM would be unusable at scale. The model is not the moat. The infrastructure around the model is the moat.

The business math

Now the arithmetic. The original estimate was 1.5 years of engineering time. The actual cost was six weeks of engineering time plus the LLM API bill. Airbnb reports the total came in at a fraction of the original estimate. But a careful student should not just celebrate the headline number — they should think about what those six weeks of engineer time were spent on. Almost none of it was “writing tests.” It was building the pipeline, tuning the prompts, reviewing the failures, and fixing the last 3% by hand. The LLM did the typing. The engineers did the architecting.

The migration looks like substitution at first — the LLM wrote code that engineers would otherwise have written. But look closer and it’s augmentation. The engineers didn’t go away. They moved up the stack: from writing individual test files to designing a pipeline, selecting context, and debugging edge cases. The total output was dramatically higher, but the engineers were still there. A recurring business question as generative AI spreads will be: does this deployment substitute for a role, or augment the people in it — and which does the company actually want?

What this says about GenAI in established businesses
Notice which kind of company pulled this off. Airbnb was not a startup tinkering with a chatbot. It was a company with more than a decade of ML experience, a mature engineering culture, well-developed monitoring and testing discipline, and engineers who already knew how to build production pipelines. The LLM was a new ingredient, but everything around it — the validation infrastructure, the retry logic, the progress tracking — was built using the same skills Airbnb’s engineers had been practicing for years. The companies best positioned to benefit quickly from generative AI are often the same ones that already invested in predictive AI. Generative AI doesn’t replace the AI Factory. It plugs into it.

11 Responsible AI at Airbnb

Every chapter in this course circles back to the same basic question: what happens when the algorithm is wrong, or right in a way the business didn’t intend? Airbnb’s version of this question is especially sharp because its AI systems touch three different constituencies — guests, hosts, and the communities those guests and hosts live in. And running through all three is a question this chapter is well-positioned to teach: who is “in the loop” when an AI system makes a consequential decision, and what does that phrase actually mean in practice?

Key concept
Human-in-the-loop (four versions at Airbnb)
A human-in-the-loop system is one where a person retains a role in reviewing or overriding what the AI recommends. But the phrase covers very different realities. Version 1: the host reviews every Smart Pricing suggestion individually. Version 2: engineers design the LLM pipeline but don’t review every output — the system validates automatically and only escalates failures. Version 3: Airbnb’s voice support AI handles common calls, but the caller can press 0 at any point to reach a human. Version 4: governments cap short-term rentals or block listings, overriding the platform’s logic from outside the system. All four are “human-in-the-loop.” They feel completely different in practice.

On the guest side: geographic bias and the filter bubble

The destination recommender is trained on guests’ past bookings. If a user has only ever booked in Europe, the model will tend to recommend European destinations. That might be exactly what the user wants. But it might also narrow their world in ways neither they nor Airbnb would choose deliberately. A destination model that learns from past behavior will systematically underweight places where the user has never been — which is, by definition, where exploration would actually take them.

A subtler version of the same issue is geographic coverage. The Airbnb Engineering team reports that autosuggest drives the biggest booking gains in regions where English is not the primary language. That’s a good outcome — it means the model is especially helpful for users who might struggle to type and spell unfamiliar destination names. But it also tells you something about the baseline: if the gains are largest in non-English markets, the experience before the model may have been systematically worse there. The model didn’t create that gap, but a responsible team asks whether the training data that produced the model might reflect it — and monitors to make sure the system is closing the gap rather than encoding it.

Airbnb notes that users can opt out of this personalization. That matters — but it also matters how many users know the option exists and how easy it is to find. Opt-out is not the same as informed consent.

On the host side: whose margin is Smart Pricing optimizing?

Risk: algorithmic price nudging
When Smart Pricing suggests lowering a host’s nightly rate to match an apparent market softening, the host faces a real choice: accept the suggestion and take a lower margin, or reject it and risk an empty calendar. A suggestion from an algorithm that sees the entire market is hard to argue against from the position of seeing just your own home. Over time, hosts who consistently override Smart Pricing may find their listings ranked lower in search results, which makes overriding the suggestion feel more costly. The “suggestion” frame is not neutral — and a host reading this chapter should be thinking about whose interests the algorithm is optimizing when its recommendations also happen to increase booking volume on the platform.

There is a structural reason to ask this question. Airbnb’s revenue depends on completed bookings. Lower prices produce more bookings. A pricing algorithm that very slightly biases toward “lower” produces more bookings at every individual decision, produces more revenue for Airbnb, and produces slightly less revenue per night for each host. None of this requires bad faith from anyone. It just requires that the objective function the algorithm optimizes is aligned with the platform’s interests rather than the hosts’. Asking whose interests a suggestion serves is one of the most basic questions of responsible AI, and it’s a question hosts have a legitimate right to ask.

This is also where the human-in-the-loop framing gets tested. Smart Pricing is technically augmentation — the host keeps final control. But the information asymmetry is enormous. The algorithm sees the entire market; the host sees one property. The host can override the suggestion, but they cannot see the data behind it, and they may not fully understand the consequences of ignoring it. A system can be human-in-the-loop by design and still feel coercive in practice. The question for a manager is not just can the human override, but does the human have enough information to override well?

On the pricing side: price discrimination

Dynamic pricing at scale raises a broader question that airlines have been living with for decades: is charging different prices to different people for the same service fair? (The Ticketmaster mini-case in §7 explored when dynamic pricing feels exploitative vs. defensible.) At Airbnb, two guests booking the same home for the same dates will generally see the same price — but prices move dramatically across dates, and hosts in certain neighborhoods may be nudged toward systematically different price curves than hosts in other neighborhoods. When the data reflects a city’s history, the algorithm can reflect that history too. A responsible pricing team monitors for this, particularly at the intersection of neighborhood demographics and dynamic pricing suggestions.

On the support side: when the AI listens first

There is a fourth AI system at Airbnb that illustrates yet another version of human-in-the-loop — and it is one most students will recognize from their own experience as customers. When a guest or host calls Airbnb for help, they do not navigate a traditional phone tree of “press 1 for billing, press 2 for reservations.” Instead, Airbnb’s ML-powered Interactive Voice Response (IVR) system asks an open-ended question: “In a few sentences, please tell us why you’re calling today.”

What happens next is a small AI pipeline. First, an automated speech recognition model transcribes what the caller says — tuned specifically for Airbnb terminology so that “listing” is not transcribed as “lifting” and “help with my stay” is not heard as “happy Christmas Day.” (Airbnb reports that domain-specific tuning cut the rate of transcription mistakes from 33% to roughly 10%.) Then an intent detection model classifies the caller’s issue into a category — cancellations, refunds, account issues — and a paraphrasing model generates a plain-language summary of the problem so the caller can confirm the system understood them. If the issue can be resolved with a help article, the system sends it via text message. If the caller wants a human, they press 0 at any point and are connected to an agent — with the AI’s understanding of the issue already attached, so the agent doesn’t start from scratch.

This is the matching problem again, in miniature. A caller has a problem they can describe but cannot classify. The system has hundreds of help articles and dozens of agent teams. The AI’s job is to infer what the caller needs from messy, noisy, spoken language and route them to the right resolution — just as the destination recommender infers where a guest wants to travel from messy behavioral signals. The pattern is the same; the domain is different.

What makes this a useful human-in-the-loop example is that the human’s role is opt-in escalation. The system tries to handle the easy cases automatically. The human agent is not reviewing every AI decision — they are standing by for the cases the AI cannot resolve or the caller does not trust it to handle. This is Version 3 from the concept box above, and it is increasingly the default pattern for customer-facing AI: automate the common path, keep a human available for the exceptions. The responsible-AI question is whether the system makes it genuinely easy to reach the human, or whether the “press 0” option is buried under enough friction that most callers give up and accept the automated answer.

Beyond the platform: the global backlash against short-term rentals

Every AI system in this chapter optimizes for matching — connecting a guest to a destination, a host to a price, a caller to a resolution, an engineer to working code. And by its own metrics, the matching works. Bookings go up. Conversion improves. Revenue grows. But there is a constituency that does not appear anywhere in the model’s objective function: the people who live in the neighborhoods where those bookings happen and who never signed up to be part of the platform at all.

This is not a hypothetical concern. Across the world, cities are pushing back — hard — against the growth of short-term rentals, and Airbnb has become a focal point of that backlash. The anger is not just about housing costs. A study from Trinity College Dublin found that in London, each additional Airbnb tourist per 1,000 residents increased complaints against tourists by 2.2%, and that higher Airbnb penetration was associated with declining neighborhood quality and reduced social capital — measured, strikingly, by the number of charitable organizations in the area. The issue is not just economic. It is social.

Global regulatory response
In Mexico City, the city’s Planning Institute estimates that roughly 100,000 people have left the city because they can no longer afford rent. In 2025, the city restricted Airbnb units to no more than 180 nights per year. In Barcelona — which received 26.1 million tourists in 2024, over 15 times its population — the city announced plans to close all 10,000 apartments licensed as short-term rentals by 2028. Protesters sprayed tourists with water pistols and set off firecrackers in widely covered demonstrations. In Spain nationally, the government ordered Airbnb to block more than 65,000 listings for rule violations in 2025, and later fined the company €64 million. Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez declared that “there are too many Airbnbs and not enough homes.” EU-wide rules for short-term rental platforms take effect in May 2026, giving member states new tools to require registration, enforce limits, and collect data. In Venice, a €5–€10 entry fee now applies to day-trippers during peak season. In Berlin, more than 8,100 vacation apartments have been returned to the housing market since the city’s regulations took effect. In New York City, it is now illegal to rent an entire apartment for fewer than 30 days unless the host is present. The pattern is global: London, Paris, Amsterdam, Vienna, Prague, Lisbon, Edinburgh, and dozens of other cities have all imposed or tightened restrictions. In June 2025, coordinated protests took place across Spain, Portugal, and Italy on the same weekend.

There is a painful irony here worth naming. Airbnb was born in the 2008 financial crisis, when ordinary people needed extra income to hold onto their apartments. The platform grew because it helped people in economic distress. Two decades later, the same platform is being blamed for creating economic distress — pricing people out of the neighborhoods that Airbnb hosts originally needed help staying in. The company that began as a lifeline for renters is now, in many cities, seen as a threat to them.

Airbnb’s leadership has pushed back on the framing. CEO Brian Chesky has argued publicly that in Barcelona, housing prices rose 60% over the past decade while Airbnb listings actually decreased, calling the platform a “convenient scapegoat for a failed policy and deep, long-standing housing issues.” Chesky points out that in Barcelona, around 70% of tourists are cruise ship passengers or hotel guests — not Airbnb users. And there is research to support the idea that short-term rentals are only one factor: a study published in Marketing Science found that a 1% increase in Airbnb listings in a zip code is associated with only a 0.018% increase in rents — a real but small effect. The deeper structural issue in most cities is chronic underbuilding, not vacation rentals per se.

But for this course, the interesting question is not “is Airbnb the cause of the housing crisis?” — it clearly isn’t the only cause. The interesting question is: what happens when an AI system’s objective function does not include the people most affected by it?

Key concept
Externalities and the objective function
Every AI system optimizes for an objective function. Smart Pricing optimizes for bookings. The destination recommender optimizes for conversion. Neither includes “impact on local housing supply” in its objective function, because displaced residents are not platform users. In economics, costs borne by people outside a transaction are called externalities. The global backlash is what happens when those externalities grow large enough for governments to intervene. The lesson: choosing what to optimize for is itself a decision with ethical and political consequences.

This connects directly to the human-in-the-loop concept we introduced at the top of this section. When Mexico City caps rentals at 180 nights, or when Spain blocks 65,000 listings, those governments are inserting themselves into the loop — from outside the system, on behalf of people the system was never designed to serve. That is Version 4 of human-in-the-loop: regulatory override. It is not elegant, and it is often blunt. But when the people most affected by an AI system have no other way to be heard, regulation becomes the loop.

On the engineering side: the LLM’s code was correct, but was it right?

The test migration in §10 is a governance story in miniature. The engineers built validation into every stage — a test either passes or it doesn’t, a file either compiles or it doesn’t. That worked because the task had hard, objective correctness checks. Not every LLM deployment does. When a generative model is used to draft a customer email, summarize a legal document, or write code that will ship to production, the question “did it work?” becomes much harder to answer mechanically. The discipline Airbnb applied to this project — validation at every step, automated retries, human review of the long tail — should be the default assumption for how LLMs get deployed in serious business contexts. It is not how they are currently deployed in most companies.

And there is a subtler issue worth naming. The engineers who reviewed the last 3% of files were checking whether the code worked — not whether it was written well, not whether it followed the team’s conventions, not whether a junior engineer could read and maintain it later. Functional correctness is the easiest thing to verify about code. Readability, maintainability, and style are much harder, and no automated test catches them. When a company uses an LLM to write thousands of files at once, it gains speed but risks accumulating a codebase that technically works but that humans struggle to understand. The human-in-the-loop in this case was in the loop for correctness. Whether anyone was in the loop for quality is a different question — and one that will recur as GenAI is deployed more broadly inside engineering organizations.

The through-line
Across all of these domains — guest personalization, host pricing, voice support, community impact, and engineering — the pattern is the same. An AI system does what it was built to do, and does it well. The hard questions are not about whether the system works. They are about who it works for, who it doesn’t, and who gets to decide. Those are not engineering questions. They are management questions, and they are the ones this course is training you to ask.

12 Competitive Advantage: The Two-Sided Data Moat

Why can’t a competitor just copy all of this? Airbnb’s engineering team has published detailed descriptions of every system in this chapter. The models are not secret. The architectures are not proprietary. And yet the advantage compounds.

  • Two-sided data, not one-sided. Hotels.com knows about rooms. Booking.com knows about standardized hotel rooms. Social platforms know about users. Airbnb knows about both sides of a matching problem at once — millions of unique homes and hundreds of millions of guests interacting with them. Both streams of data feed every model in this chapter, and neither stream can be bought or shortcut by a competitor.
  • Every interaction is training data for multiple systems. A single search session teaches the destination recommender about travel intent, the clustering algorithm about which homes compete for similar audiences, and Smart Pricing about how demand is evolving. The marginal cost of generating this compound signal is zero; it happens automatically, on every visit.
  • Mature AI infrastructure makes new AI cheap to add. Part III of this chapter is only possible because Parts I and II already exist. Airbnb’s engineers knew how to build validation pipelines, monitor model behavior, and run tuning loops because they had been doing it with predictive models for a decade. A newcomer can buy an LLM subscription tomorrow; they cannot buy that decade of institutional learning.
  • The flywheel runs in both directions. More guests make the destination recommender smarter, which converts better, which brings more hosts, who supply more listings, which attracts more guests. Competitors trying to break into either side face a cold-start problem of their own: they can’t serve guests well until they have hosts, and they can’t attract hosts until they have guests.

The moat is not the algorithm. It is the data, the infrastructure, the organizational learning, and the compounding feedback loop between two sides of a marketplace that reinforce each other. Everything else is copyable. That combination is not.

If you find yourself evaluating a company’s AI strategy — as a consultant, an investor, a product manager, or a competitor — the question is not “do they have AI?” Every company will have AI. The question is: do they have the compounding data, the two-sided feedback loop, and the organizational infrastructure that make their AI systems get better every day without anyone deciding to improve them? That is the moat. Everything else is a feature.

Discussion questions

These work well as written assignments or in-class discussion prompts. Questions 2, 3, and 7 tend to generate the most debate.

  1. Two sides, one platform — advantage or risk? Is Airbnb’s decision to optimize for both sides of its marketplace simultaneously a competitive advantage or an ethical risk? Take a position and defend it using specific evidence from the destination recommender, Smart Pricing, or both.
  2. Whose margin is Smart Pricing optimizing? When Smart Pricing suggests a lower price to a host during a quiet period, whose interests is that algorithm actually serving? Argue it through — is it the host’s, Airbnb’s, the guest’s, or some combination? Would a host-first version of the algorithm look different?
  3. Who was underserved before the model? Airbnb’s destination recommender performs best in regions where English is not the primary language. What does that tell you about which users were underserved before the model existed — and what does it say about how Airbnb should measure the success of a recommender like this?
  4. When is interpretability worth losing accuracy? Smart Pricing could have used pure ML and likely would have been somewhat more accurate than its hybrid approach. Instead, Airbnb chose a hybrid that gave up some raw accuracy in exchange for interpretability. Where is the line? Pick a business context where a manager should insist on interpretability even at a real cost to accuracy, and one where they shouldn’t.
  5. Travel intent as text. Airbnb treats user actions as “tokens” the way a language model treats words. What are the risks of borrowing a framework designed for text and applying it to human travel intent? Where could this abstraction break — and what would the failure mode look like in the product?
  6. 1.5 years to 6 weeks. Airbnb’s engineers originally estimated the test migration at 1.5 years of work. They finished it in 6 weeks with LLMs. What should managers conclude from that number — and what should they not conclude? Under what conditions would you generalize this result to other teams or other kinds of work, and when would you resist?
  7. Filter bubble or personalization? The destination model learns from a user’s past bookings. A user who has only ever booked in Europe will see mostly European recommendations. Is the model personalizing correctly — or narrowing the user’s world? Argue both sides.
