1 Technology Has Always Had an Environmental Cost

This chapter is about AI and the environment. But to understand why AI’s environmental footprint matters, we need to go back a bit — because the tech industry’s environmental cost didn’t start with ChatGPT. It has been growing for decades, and we’re all part of it whether we realize it or not.

E-waste: the cost of constant upgrades

Every phone we’ve traded in, every laptop that stopped getting updates, every pair of earbuds with a dead battery — those are e-waste. In 2022, the world generated a record 62 million tonnes of electronic waste, up 82% from 2010. That’s enough to fill 1.55 million trucks in a line around the equator. Only about 22% was properly recycled. The rest went to landfills or unregulated dump sites — often in lower-income countries — where toxic materials like lead and mercury leach into soil and water. By 2030, e-waste is projected to hit 82 million tonnes per year, and the pace of generation is growing five times faster than recycling capacity.

Source
The Global E-waste Monitor 2024
Published by UNITAR and the ITU, this is the definitive global report on electronic waste. The AI buildout is adding to this problem: every server, GPU, and networking component in a data center eventually becomes e-waste, and the current replacement cycle for AI hardware is accelerating as newer, more powerful chips come out.

Streaming and social media: the footprint you already have

Here’s something most students don’t think about: every TikTok video we scroll past, every Netflix episode we binge, every Spotify playlist we stream — all of that runs on the same data center infrastructure that powers AI. And it’s not just the video file being sent to your phone. When you open TikTok, the app isn’t showing you a simple list of videos. It’s running a massive recommendation engine that processes your watch time, likes, shares, scroll speed, and hundreds of other signals — in real time, for every single user, on every single swipe — to decide what to show you next. TikTok’s estimated annual carbon footprint is roughly 50 million tonnes of CO₂ — comparable to the entire country of Greece. And TikTok users spend an average of 46 minutes per day on the app, all of it feeding that recommendation engine.

Netflix is similar but better documented. Netflix’s own estimate, based on Carbon Trust research, puts the footprint of one hour of streaming at roughly 55 grams of CO₂. The IEA’s analysis puts it lower, around 36 grams per hour. That sounds small — until you multiply it across hundreds of millions of subscribers. Netflix alone produced over 5 million tonnes of CO₂ from streaming in 2024.

The point isn’t to make anyone feel guilty about watching Netflix or scrolling TikTok. It’s to recognize that we all already have a data center footprint. The infrastructure was built for us long before generative AI came along. AI is adding to it.

So what changed with generative AI?

The key difference is how hard the computers have to work. When you stream a Netflix show, the data center pulls a pre-recorded video file from storage and sends it to your device. That’s relatively simple. When TikTok runs its recommendation engine, it’s doing more work — processing signals and ranking content in real time. But when you ask ChatGPT a question, the data center is running billions of calculations to generate a completely new response that has never existed before. A single ChatGPT query uses roughly 10 times more electricity than a Google search. An AI-generated image uses roughly the same energy as charging your phone.

Think of it as a spectrum of computational intensity: streaming a cached video < running a recommendation algorithm < generating a novel AI response. Generative AI sits at the heavy end. It didn’t create the data center energy problem — but it dramatically accelerated it by adding a workload that is far more compute-intensive per request than anything the infrastructure was originally built for. That’s the context for everything that follows.

2 The Number Everyone Quotes — and Why It’s the Wrong One

When critics talk about AI’s environmental footprint, they almost always start with training — the upfront cost of building a model. And the numbers sound scary. Training GPT-3 used about 5.4 million liters of water. About 700,000 liters evaporated cooling the servers; the rest went to generating the electricity that kept them running.

But here’s the thing: California’s almond industry uses 1–2 trillion liters of water every year. U.S. golf courses use a similar amount. One day of almond production could cover the water cost of training GPT-3 hundreds of thousands of times over. So if you only look at training, AI doesn’t seem that bad.

Key Concept
Training vs. Inference
Training is the one-time process of building an AI model — feeding it data and adjusting it until it works. Think of it like constructing a factory. Inference is what happens every time someone uses that model — every prompt, every query, every image request. Think of it like running the factory 24/7. Training is a one-time capital expense. Inference is the electric bill that never stops. The industry prefers to talk about training because it’s a fixed, known cost. Inference is the part that grows without any ceiling.

If the environmental critique of AI stops at training, the almond comparison wins. The honest answer is that training costs, for the models where published figures exist, are not categorically worse than several things society accepts without a public reckoning.

But training is a one-time expense. What happens after training — the part you interact with every day — is a different story entirely.

3 Training Is the Headline. Inference Is the Bill.

Every time someone asks ChatGPT to write a poem, draft a cover letter, summarize a meeting, or generate an image, the meter runs. One query uses a tiny amount of water and energy. But when hundreds of millions of people do it every day, those tiny amounts add up fast. The IEA (International Energy Agency) flagged data-center electricity as one of the fastest-growing energy demands on Earth this decade.

Here’s the math:

Inference at Scale
ChatGPT has roughly 800 million weekly users. Picture each one asking the model ten short text questions a day for a year. At OpenAI’s own published figure of ~0.3 mL of water per query, that is approximately 880 million liters of water a year. Researchers estimate the true figure is 5–50× higher once off-site cooling for electricity generation is included, yielding a range of 4.4 to 44 billion liters per year — from one product alone.

The almond comparison works if you only look at training. It falls apart the moment you factor in inference at scale. Training happens once. Inference happens every second of every day, and the companies are building more capacity as fast as they can. Anthropic’s April 2026 multi-gigawatt compute deal with Google and Broadcom — scheduled to start in 2027 — is pre-committing infrastructure for demand that hasn’t even arrived yet.

The same math works for carbon

Take ChatGPT’s 800 million weekly users again. Imagine each one generates a single AI image, just once. One AI image takes about 11.5 watt-hours of electricity — roughly what it takes to charge your phone. Multiply that by 800 million people and you get 9.2 gigawatt-hours of electricity for one round of images. At global average grid emissions (~400g of CO₂ per kWh), that’s roughly 3,700 tonnes of CO₂ — the same as 2,500 cars driving for a full year. For one round of pictures.

So if the real cost is this large, why don’t the companies’ sustainability reports say so? Because of how carbon accounting works — and what it lets you leave out.

4 The Carbon Accounting Gap: Scopes 1, 2, and 3

When companies report their environmental impact, carbon emissions get sorted into three “scopes.” If you go into any career that touches sustainability, ESG reporting, or corporate responsibility, you’ll use this framework. Here’s how it works:

ScopeWhat it coversAI exampleTypically reported?
Scope 1Direct emissions from owned sourcesDiesel backup generators at a data centerYes
Scope 2Indirect emissions from purchased energyElectricity bought to run serversYes
Scope 3All other supply chain emissionsEmbodied carbon in chips, servers, buildings, construction materialsSelectively

Scopes 1 and 2 are the numbers companies like to highlight — and they’re making real progress there, investing billions in wind and solar. Scope 3 is where the uncomfortable truth lives. It’s harder to measure, harder to control, and much harder to spin as a success story. It’s also where most of AI’s environmental impact actually is.

Key Concept
Embodied Carbon
The emissions released to make something before it’s ever turned on. A server has embodied carbon from mining metals, manufacturing chips, assembling components, and shipping the finished product. A data center has embodied carbon from all the concrete, steel, and hardware inside it. Embodied carbon is a Scope 3 emission. It’s real, it’s large, and it’s the part most companies leave out of their headline numbers.

Here’s a real example. Microsoft’s 2024 Environmental Sustainability Report showed Scope 3 emissions up 30.9% from 2020. Why? Their own explanation: building more data centers, plus all the embodied carbon in the building materials, semiconductors, servers, and racks that go inside them. Microsoft doesn’t directly blame AI for the increase — but the data center buildout is clearly tracking AI demand.

The UK Revision
The UK government’s original projection put AI data center emissions at 0.05% of Britain’s total carbon output. After a Foxglove and Carbon Brief investigation in early 2026, the revised projection jumped to 34–123 million tonnes of CO₂ by 2035 — hundreds of times higher than the original estimate. That is the pattern: the industry reports what can be framed as achievement. It omits what can only be framed as cost.

The Scope 1/2/3 framework tells you where to look. But even with the best accounting, there’s a more fundamental gap: nobody has done a complete environmental audit of any major AI model. Period.

5 The Missing Lifecycle Assessment

No independent, full lifecycle assessment of any current frontier AI model exists.

Key Concept
Lifecycle Assessment (LCA)
A standardized environmental audit that tracks a product’s total impact from cradle to grave — raw materials, manufacturing, use, and disposal. It’s the gold standard for answering “what does this thing actually cost the environment?” For AI, a proper LCA would cover everything from mining the metals for chips to shutting down the servers at end of life, including the water and energy of every single query over the model’s lifetime. No one has done this for any current frontier AI model.

Some researchers — like Alexandra Sasha Luccioni, Emma Strubell, and Pengfei Li — have done partial assessments of smaller or older models. But for the big models that are actually driving the current building boom (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini), the companies have either partially cooperated or declined to participate in independent assessments.

Why This Matters for Business
The absence of lifecycle assessments is not just a research gap — it is a procurement risk. Organizations purchasing AI services at scale are making vendor selection decisions without the environmental cost data they would demand for any other major infrastructure investment. Until credible independent LCAs exist, every critic argues from partial figures and every defender argues from selective ones.

Even without a full LCA, though, there’s one question we can answer right now: is all this usage even worth the cost? That depends on what the AI is actually being used for.

6 The Waste Question: Not All Prompts Are Equal

The Slow AI article points out something that should bother anyone thinking about this problem: a huge amount of AI usage produces output that nobody ever reads. AI writes LinkedIn posts. Other AI summarizes those posts. The “readers” are bots. The content is fake. But the water, electricity, and carbon emissions are completely real.

“The most resource-intensive technology ever built is being used in large part to generate content nobody asked for, read by nobody, saved by nobody, remembered by nobody. An air-conditioned graveyard of text.” — Dr. Sam Illingworth, Slow AI

This framing introduces what we might call a waste factor — the proportion of AI-generated output that is never meaningfully read, used, or acted upon. Consider the difference:

Low Waste Factor
High-value use cases
Accessibility tools that let a disabled user navigate the internet. Medical research that accelerates drug discovery. Climate modeling that improves prediction accuracy. Student learning that deepens understanding of course material. Same water, same energy, same carbon per query — but the output is read, used, and matters.
High Waste Factor
Low-value use cases
Auto-generated marketing emails opened by no one. AI-written LinkedIn posts summarized by AI for AI. Decorative image generation viewed once and forgotten. Bulk content produced to fill SEO-optimized pages that humans never visit. Same water, same energy, same carbon per query — but the output evaporates.
The Caveat That Matters
The Slow AI article is careful to note: the critique is aimed at companies, infrastructure, and policy — not individual users. Disabled users, for whom AI tools remove real barriers that society has never funded through any other route, are the last people anyone should blame for a system they did not build. The structural question is whether companies should be required to disclose per-query environmental costs so that organizations and individuals can make informed choices.

This calculator was built with AI assistance. The prompts used to create it, the server that generated the code, and the energy powering the device you’re reading it on all have environmental costs. We’re not hiding that — it’s the point. The question isn’t whether to use AI. It’s whether each use justifies its cost, and whether the companies providing it should be required to tell you what that cost is.

The calculator above makes the problem concrete: even modest individual usage adds up fast at platform scale. So what is the industry actually doing about it? The answer is a mix of genuinely innovative engineering and some approaches that trade one environmental problem for another.

7 Emerging Solutions: How the Market Is Responding

Pointing out the problem is important, but it’s only half the story. The market is already responding with several different approaches to powering AI more sustainably. Each one involves real tradeoffs. As a business student, the question to ask isn’t “which solution is best?” but “what is each approach betting on, and what risks does it leave on the table?”

Solution 1 Wave-Powered Floating Data Centers

Panthalassa, based in Vancouver, Washington, is developing self-propelled floating data centers that generate electricity from wave energy, process AI workloads onboard, and transmit results by satellite. No anchor, no undersea cable, no municipal water supply. The units navigate autonomously — “like a little Roomba, except it’s enormous.” Construction of the Ocean-3 units is underway with an expected launch around August 2026.

The business case is straightforward: AI companies get power faster than land-based data center construction allows, with zero carbon emissions during operation and no competition for grid capacity. Panthalassa says it has all the private funding it needs because the value proposition to AI companies is speed and cleanliness.

Key question
Can wave-powered data centers actually work at scale?
Cost per compute-hour is unproven. Reliability in adverse ocean conditions is untested at commercial scale. Satellite transmission adds latency for time-sensitive workloads. Maintenance logistics in open ocean are unknown. The technology is pre-revenue — promising, but with no track record.

Solution 2 Nuclear: Restarts and Small Modular Reactors

Restart Path
Microsoft & Three Mile Island
Microsoft is paying $1.6 billion to restart Three Mile Island’s Unit 1 reactor (now renamed the Crane Clean Energy Center), securing 20 years of steady, always-on carbon-free power. Constellation Energy estimates reopening the existing facility is at least 10× cheaper than building new, with a ~3-year timeline vs. the decade typically required for new nuclear. The reactor ran safely from 1974 to 2019; only Unit 2 had the 1979 partial meltdown.
New Build Path
Google & Kairos Power SMRs
Google is funding Kairos Power to build small modular reactors (SMRs) that use fuel pebbles instead of traditional fuel rods. Each pebble — roughly golf-ball sized — contains as much energy capacity as four tons of coal, with zero CO₂ during operation. Google anticipates the first reactor online by 2030. Kairos is building three demonstration plants in Oak Ridge, Tennessee.
The Skeptic’s Case
Sharon Squassoni, a George Washington University professor who spent 15 years researching nuclear safety for the federal government, argues that SMRs remain commercially unproven: “There’s only one problem with small modular reactors: they don’t really exist.” She predicts tech companies will find the timeline too long and the cost too high, and will ultimately turn to burning fossil fuels. Nuclear also still produces waste requiring long-term storage.

Solution 3 Off-Grid Private Power Plants

The Trump administration has encouraged tech companies to build their own power plants with expedited permitting. Several executives have pledged to do so. The appeal is speed: when Elon Musk was told connecting his xAI Colossus facility in Tennessee to the grid would take years, he brought in mobile natural gas generators and had the world’s largest AI training data center operational in four months.

The Tradeoff
Speed and clean power are, at present, in direct tension. According to Cleanview, a single OpenAI data center planned for New Mexico would emit 14 million tons of CO₂ per year if powered by natural gas — equivalent to the entire state’s emissions reductions over the previous 20 years. As Cleanview’s CEO told NPR: “A single data center would wipe out 20 years of climate progress.”

Solution 4 Grid + Renewables

The less dramatic but potentially most impactful solution is simply building more renewable energy and connecting it to the grid. And here, AI companies are spending enormous amounts of money. Amazon is now the world’s largest corporate buyer of renewable energy, with more than 500 solar and wind projects globally. Google signed a $20 billion partnership with Intersect Power and TPG Rise Climate to build data centers co-located with solar, wind, and battery storage, with the first phase expected to be operational by 2026. Microsoft has added hundreds of megawatts of solar capacity across Illinois, Michigan, and Missouri specifically for AI data centers.

By the end of Q3 2024, the U.S. data center sector alone had contracted 50 gigawatts of clean energy — 29 GW from solar and 13 GW from wind. Data center capital expenditure reached $770 billion in 2025, surpassing investment in upstream oil and gas for the first time.

The catch
Renewables are intermittent. AI is not.
Solar doesn’t work at night. Wind doesn’t blow on demand. But AI data centers need constant, uninterrupted power — 24/7, 365 days a year. The gap is being filled by grid-scale battery storage (companies like Fluence Energy are building systems specifically for this), but storage technology is still catching up to the scale of demand. Until it does, natural gas remains the most common backup — which is why even “renewable-powered” data centers often still have a carbon footprint.

Externality The Consumer Cost Problem

The energy question is not abstract for communities near data centers. Data centers are contributing to rising utility bills in at least 13 U.S. states, according to the Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis. In Georgia, average residential electricity bills have climbed from roughly $150 to $225 per month during the same period that data center construction accelerated. A Bloomberg analysis found Americans living near data centers are paying up to 267% more per month for energy than five years ago.

Key Concept
Externality
A cost (or benefit) that lands on someone who didn’t choose it. If a data center moves into your town and your electricity bill doubles, that’s a negative externality — you’re paying part of the cost of AI infrastructure you didn’t ask for and may not benefit from. When Georgia Power gives data centers discounted rates, the gap gets filled by residential customers. That’s a textbook example of cost externalization — and a concept you’ll see in every economics and strategy class.
Policy in Motion
In April 2026, Maine’s Governor Janet Mills vetoed what would have been the first state-level ban on new data center construction. Georgia Power announced a rate freeze and agreed to use revenue from large customers to offset residential costs. The regulatory landscape is actively forming — and business students entering the workforce in the next two years will be making decisions inside it.

Even if every data center ran on perfectly clean energy, the water, hardware, and construction costs would remain. The deeper question — should companies be required to tell you the environmental cost of each query, regardless of the power source? — stays unanswered.

That question leads directly to regulation. If the market isn’t going to solve disclosure on its own — and so far it hasn’t — then policy has to. Here’s where things stand.

8 The Regulatory Landscape

AI regulation in general has been slow. Environmental regulation of AI specifically is even further behind. But things are moving in three places your careers will intersect with.

United States: Proposed but stalled

The most significant proposal is Senator Ed Markey’s Artificial Intelligence Environmental Impacts Act, introduced in February 2024. The bill would direct NIST to develop standards for measuring AI’s environmental impact, create a voluntary reporting framework for AI developers, and require the EPA to conduct a comprehensive study. It has bipartisan support in concept, but has not been enacted. A GAO report released in April 2025 confirmed the bill’s premise — that AI poses significant environmental impacts and that information gaps need to be addressed — but momentum has stalled.

Meanwhile, the regulatory direction has moved the opposite way. In January 2025, an executive order titled “Removing Barriers to American AI Innovation” called for agencies to rescind policies that might slow AI development. Senator Markey wrote to EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin in September 2025 opposing a decision to let major polluting facilities — including data centers — begin pre-construction without air quality permits. A separate bill, the Protect American AI Act of 2026, would limit the ability of environmental lawsuits to block data center construction.

The Tension
The US regulatory picture is a tug-of-war: one side wants environmental disclosure and accountability for AI infrastructure; the other wants to remove environmental review requirements to accelerate AI buildout. Business students should understand that this tension is not going away — and procurement decisions made today will be judged against whichever framework wins.

European Union: Ahead, but with gaps

The EU is further along. Its approach combines two tracks:

The Energy Efficiency Directive (EED) already requires data centers with 500+ kW of IT power to report energy consumption, water footprint, capacity utilization, and renewable energy usage annually to a European database. The next reporting deadline is May 15, 2026. Germany went further in its national implementation: data centers opening after July 2026 must achieve minimum waste heat recovery thresholds, and 100% renewable electricity is required from January 2027.

The EU AI Act (entered into force August 2024) lists environmental protection as a stated goal and requires providers of general-purpose AI models to report energy consumption during training. It also calls for voluntary codes of conduct covering energy-efficient programming and AI’s environmental impact. However, environmental provisions were significantly weakened during negotiations — mandatory sustainability impact assessments were dropped from the final text. The European Commission’s first report on progress toward energy-efficient AI standards is not due until August 2028.

Coming in 2026
The European Commission is preparing a Data Centre Energy Efficiency Package and a Cloud and AI Development Act, both expected in 2026. The goal: carbon-neutral data centers by 2030, with a plan to triple EU data center capacity in 5–7 years — but only if new facilities meet requirements on energy efficiency, water efficiency, and recycling of materials. The EU is trying to expand AI infrastructure and regulate its environmental impact at the same time. Whether those two goals are compatible is one of the central policy questions of the next decade.

State and local level

In the absence of federal action in the US, states are stepping in. Maine’s governor vetoed what would have been the first state-level ban on new data center construction in April 2026. Oregon’s POWER Act (August 2025) established special electricity rates for data centers that incentivize efficiency. Multiple states are considering disclosure requirements. For business students, the takeaway is that regulatory compliance for AI infrastructure is becoming a patchwork — similar to data privacy before GDPR — and companies operating across jurisdictions will need to plan for the most restrictive requirements.

The Scale of What’s Coming
A Cornell University study covered by NPR mapped AI’s projected environmental impact state by state. Their findings: by 2030, AI growth in the US would add 24 to 44 million metric tons of CO₂ to the atmosphere and consume as much water as 6 to 10 million Americans use in a year. Their conclusion: this trajectory puts the tech industry’s own climate goals out of reach. As the lead researcher noted, “If we build AI in the right place, on a clean power grid and with efficient cooling technologies, it could really grow without blowing past water and climate limits.” Location and infrastructure choices matter — and those are business decisions.

Regulation will shape the rules over time. But you don’t have to wait for policy to catch up — some of the most impactful decisions are ones you can make right now.

9 Model Selection as a Business Decision

One of the most practical decisions you’ll make in your career is which AI model to use for which task. This is both a cost decision and an environmental one — and the two point in the same direction.

Key Concept
Right-sizing your model
You don’t charter a 747 to fly across town. You don’t need a frontier model to autocomplete a Slack message. Right-sizing means matching the model’s capability to the task’s actual requirements. A small model (like Claude Haiku or GPT-4o mini) uses roughly one-tenth the energy of a large model (like Claude Opus or GPT-4.5) per query. For routine tasks — drafting emails, summarizing meetings, answering FAQ-style questions — the smaller model gives equivalent results at a fraction of the environmental and financial cost.

This isn’t just about being green — it’s a competitive advantage. Companies that run inference more efficiently have lower operating costs and a better ESG story. Microsoft, Google, and Anthropic are all investing in inference efficiency not primarily because they’re environmentalists, but because cheaper inference means higher margins. When your company evaluates AI tools, the environmental argument and the budget argument are the same argument.

Use a small model when
The task is routine
Autocompleting messages, simple Q&A, formatting text, translating short passages, classifying support tickets, generating standard reports. These tasks don’t benefit from a larger model’s reasoning depth — and a small model is 3–10× cheaper and faster.
Use a large model when
The task requires deep reasoning
Complex analysis, multi-step research, creative strategy, code architecture, legal document review, scientific reasoning. These tasks genuinely benefit from the larger model’s capability — and the higher cost per query is justified by the quality of the output.

10 What to Ask in a Vendor Meeting

Within a few years of graduating, many of you will be in meetings where your company is evaluating AI vendors. Right now, almost nobody asks about environmental cost in those meetings. There’s no standard checklist. Here are five questions that would change that — and that you can bring to any procurement conversation:

1️⃣
Do you publish per-query energy and water data?
If the vendor can’t tell you the energy cost of a single query on their platform, they either don’t measure it or don’t want to share it. Either answer is a red flag. Ask for the number by model tier and by region.
2️⃣
What percentage of your energy comes from renewable sources?
Watch for the difference between “we buy renewable energy certificates” (which can be purchased separately from actual clean power) and “our data centers are powered by renewables 24/7.” The first is accounting. The second is physics.
3️⃣
Where are your data centers located?
A data center in a water-stressed region (Arizona, parts of Texas) has a fundamentally different environmental profile than one in the Pacific Northwest. Siting is a policy choice, and it matters.
4️⃣
Do you report Scope 3 emissions?
If the vendor only reports Scope 1 and 2, they’re leaving out the biggest part of their footprint — the embodied carbon in hardware, construction, and their supply chain. Ask specifically about Scope 3.
5️⃣
Can you help us right-size our model usage?
A good vendor will offer tiered model access and help you route simple tasks to smaller models. If the vendor’s pricing only incentivizes using the largest model for everything, they’re not aligned with your cost or environmental goals.
Why This List Matters
These questions don’t exist in most procurement checklists today. That’s the disclosure gap in action. By asking them, you signal to vendors that environmental cost is a factor in your purchasing decision — and over time, that changes what vendors choose to measure and report. Procurement is where individual choice becomes structural pressure.

11 Where This Is Going

It would be easy to finish this chapter feeling paralyzed. The numbers are big, the disclosure is poor, and the regulatory landscape is fragmented. But the honest picture is more complicated than “AI is bad for the environment.”

Individual responsibility is real but limited

The Slow AI article’s advice — refuse unnecessary prompts, use the smallest model that does the job, ask your employer what they procure — is sound. You should do those things. But as Sam Illingworth writes, “the fight is structural. It lives with procurement departments, with policy, and with the companies that still will not tell us the numbers.” Your personal prompt footprint is a fraction of a fraction. The decisions that matter are made by the companies building the infrastructure, the policymakers setting the rules, and the procurement officers signing the contracts. That’s the level where your business education becomes relevant.

We are at the beginning

Everything in this chapter describes a technology and an industry that is less than a decade old and scaling faster than any infrastructure buildout in history. The solutions we covered — wave-powered floating data centers, nuclear restarts, small modular reactors, off-grid renewable microgrids — did not exist as serious commercial propositions two years ago. Some will fail. Others will reshape the energy landscape.

AI as a catalyst for clean energy
There is a genuine possibility that AI’s energy appetite becomes the forcing function that accelerates renewable energy adoption. The IEA’s 2025 Energy and AI report projects that half of global data center demand growth will be met by renewables by 2035, supported by storage and grid improvements. Data centers now account for 43% of all corporate clean energy procurement. AI itself is also being used to optimize grids, forecast renewable generation, and reduce energy waste — the IEA estimates that AI-based fault detection can reduce grid outage durations by 30–50%. The irony would be remarkable: the technology creating the energy problem could become the technology that solves it.

The question for business students

The question this chapter asks is not “should we use AI?” — that question has already been answered by the market. The question is: under what conditions, at what cost, with what disclosure, and with what accountability? You are entering careers where these decisions will be on your desk. The companies that get the answers right will have a competitive advantage. The ones that don’t will face regulatory, reputational, and financial risk.

The environmental cost of AI is not a reason to stop using it. It is a reason to demand that the companies building it tell you what it costs — and a reason to make that cost part of every business decision you make.

12 Key Terms

Terms introduced in this chapter, in the order they appear.

Training
The one-time process of building an AI model by feeding it data and adjusting its parameters. Think of it as a capital expense — you pay it once, and then every future query runs on what you built.
Inference
The ongoing process of running a trained model every time someone submits a prompt. The recurring operating cost — per query it is tiny, in aggregate it compounds without ceiling.
Scope 1 emissions
Direct emissions from sources a company owns or controls, such as diesel generators on site.
Scope 2 emissions
Indirect emissions from purchased energy — the electricity a data center buys from the grid.
Scope 3 emissions
All other supply chain emissions: embodied carbon in hardware, construction materials, logistics, and everything upstream and downstream of direct operations.
Embodied carbon
The total carbon emissions released to manufacture something before it is ever used — from mining rare earth metals to assembling servers to pouring concrete for data center foundations.
Lifecycle assessment (LCA)
A standardized environmental audit that tracks a product’s total impact from raw materials through manufacturing, use, and disposal — the gold standard for answering “what does this actually cost the planet?” No independent LCA exists for any current frontier AI model.
Waste factor
The proportion of AI-generated output that is never meaningfully read, used, or acted upon. An accessibility tool has a waste factor near zero; an auto-generated marketing email opened by nobody has a waste factor near one.
Externality
A cost (or benefit) imposed on a party who did not choose to incur it. Rising energy bills for communities near data centers are a negative externality of AI infrastructure buildout.
Small modular reactor (SMR)
A nuclear reactor designed to be smaller, factory-built, and deployable in modular configurations. Proposed as carbon-free baseload power for data centers, but not yet commercially operational.
Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)
A long-term contract where a company commits to buying electricity from a specific power source — such as a wind farm or nuclear plant — often at a fixed price. Used by tech companies to claim renewable energy use.
Per-query disclosure
The idea that AI companies should report the energy, water, and carbon cost of each query, broken down by region and model size. Currently proposed in the U.S. AI Environmental Impacts Act and UK AI (Regulation) Bill.
Right-sizing
Matching the AI model’s capability to the task’s actual requirements. Using a small model for routine tasks and reserving large models for complex reasoning reduces both cost and environmental impact.
Energy Efficiency Directive (EED)
An EU law requiring large data centers (500+ kW) to report their energy use, water footprint, and renewable energy percentage every year. Currently the most comprehensive mandatory environmental reporting rule for AI infrastructure anywhere in the world.
Renewable Energy Certificate (REC)
A tradable certificate representing one megawatt-hour of renewable electricity generated. Companies can buy RECs to claim renewable energy use without physically receiving clean power — an accounting distinction that matters for evaluating vendor sustainability claims.

13 Discussion Questions

Use this chapter, the original Slow AI article, and the AI Prompt Footprint Calculator to support your thinking.

  1. Training vs. inference. Why does the chapter argue that focusing on training costs misses the bigger picture? What makes inference fundamentally different from training as an environmental concern?
  2. The waste factor. The Slow AI article describes AI-generated content consumed by no one as “an air-conditioned graveyard of text.” Should organizations think about whether a prompt is “worth it” before using AI? Who gets to decide what counts as waste — and what are the risks of making that judgment?
  3. Disclosure and trust. No independent lifecycle assessment exists for any frontier AI model. Why do you think that is? What would change if companies were required to publish per-query environmental data the way food companies publish nutrition labels?
  4. Who pays? Data centers are driving up electricity costs for nearby residents. Is that an acceptable tradeoff for the benefits AI provides? How should the costs of AI infrastructure be distributed between the companies that profit from it and the communities that host it?
  5. Right-sizing. The chapter introduces the idea that using a smaller model for simpler tasks reduces environmental cost without sacrificing quality. Can you think of examples from your own AI usage where a smaller model would have been sufficient? What barriers might prevent organizations from adopting a right-sizing policy?
