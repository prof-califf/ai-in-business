Part 1

Conceptual Understanding

35 points

Answer each question in one well-developed paragraph (five sentences minimum). These are not definition questions — they ask you to use a concept from the case to reason through a problem, make a judgment, or argue a position. Make sure you are applying the course vocabulary throughout your answers — if the chapter calls it a "cold-start problem" or "human-on-the-loop," use those terms precisely.

Tying in material across chapters shows critical thinking. Exam 2 covers Chapters 6 and 7, but the questions explicitly ask you to connect back to earlier chapters. The cases are all connected — your answers should be too.

A definition earns partial credit at best. Full marks go to answers that take a clear position, back it up with evidence from the case, and point out what makes it complicated. Use external sources — news articles, company blog posts, industry reports — alongside the chapter material to strengthen your arguments. Include URLs for any sources you reference.

Airbnb — Smart Pricing says €45 a night. The host charges €120. Whose judgment should the platform trust?
5 pts
🏠 Ch 6 · Airbnb ↗

Airbnb's Smart Pricing uses a hybrid model — combining what we know about how far in advance people typically book (lead time distributions) with machine learning — to suggest nightly prices. But the chapter is clear: the host gets the final call. An experienced host in Barcelona might look at Smart Pricing's €45 suggestion and set the price at €120 because she knows a music festival is coming that the model hasn't seen yet. Is this a feature of the system or a failure? Use the chapter's explanation of hybrid models, demand aggregations, and the prediction–decision gap to make your case. When is the host's local knowledge more valuable than the algorithm's prediction? When is the host wrong?

Then connect this to Uber (Chapter 4): Uber's surge pricing doesn't give drivers the final call — the algorithm sets the price. Airbnb does give hosts control. What does each approach gain, and what does it lose? Is there a version where the balance is wrong on either side?

1.2
Airbnb — A first-time guest opens the app. No searches. No bookings. No reviews. How does the AI know what to show them?
5 pts
🏠 Ch 6 · Airbnb ↗

Airbnb's recommendation system works by turning every guest and every listing into a set of numbers — what the chapter calls embeddings — so that similar guests and similar listings end up close together in a shared space. The system works well for returning guests because it has years of search and booking data to learn from. But a first-time user just downloaded the app. They haven't searched for anything, booked anything, or left a review. The system has nothing to go on. The chapter calls this the cold-start problem.

Explain how the cold-start problem works at Airbnb and describe at least one way the platform tries to get around it. Why is this problem harder for a two-sided marketplace (where you need to satisfy both the guest and the host) than for a platform that only serves one type of user? What goes wrong if the AI recommends badly in those first few interactions?

Then connect this to Netflix (Chapter 2): Netflix faces the same problem when a brand-new subscriber signs up — it has to recommend movies before it knows anything about the person's taste. Both companies need to make good recommendations immediately or they risk losing the user. How does the data each platform collects differ, and why does Airbnb's two-sided structure make the cold-start problem harder than Netflix's?

If it involves agents that take actions, use Epic's three levels. If it involves pricing, use the hybrid model logic. Don't just mention the concept — apply it to explain how your prototype works.

What "business case" means

A business case is not "AI is cool and this would be neat." It is an argument: here is a specific problem, here is who has it, here is how AI solves it better than the alternative, and here is what makes it worth building. Think like someone pitching to a skeptical VP who controls the budget.

Prompting tip

You will use GenAI to build your prototype in 3b — and the quality of what it produces depends on how well you prompt it. Give it your specific scenario, your target user, the functionality you need, and the constraints. Iterate: if the first output isn't right, tell it what to fix and why. Review the Writing Good Prompts guide before you start building.
