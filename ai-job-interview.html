Talking About AIIn a Job Interview

What you actually know, why it matters, and how to show it

Let’s be direct about something. The job market for new graduates right now is the hardest it has been in years. The unemployment rate for young workers is about twice the national average. Forty percent of college graduates who are working have taken jobs that do not require a degree. Software development job postings on Indeed have dropped 53% since late 2022. A CBS Sunday Morning segment in June 2026 interviewed recent graduates who described submitting hundreds of applications into what one called “some kind of void” — no responses, no rejections, just silence. One position had 300 applicants.

In that environment, almost every candidate will claim they “know AI.” Most of them will not be able to say anything specific. You can. That gap — between students who can speak concretely about AI and students who just repeat buzzwords — is one of the most useful advantages you can create for yourself right now.

This guide is about how to use what you have actually done in this course to stand out in an interview, and how to build a simple portfolio that makes your work visible before the conversation even starts.

1. The Problem With "I Know AI"

Why vague answers hurt you even when they sound fine

Most business students preparing for interviews will say something like: “I’m really interested in AI and how it’s changing business.” Interviewers hear this dozens of times a day. It signals enthusiasm but no substance. It does not differentiate you from anyone else in the pile.

The answer that gets remembered is specific. It names real companies, real concepts, and real things you actually built or analyzed. It shows that you understand how AI creates business value, not just that you are aware it exists.

✕ Forgettable
“I’ve been learning a lot about AI and I think it’s really going to change how companies operate. I’m excited to apply that in a business context.”
✓ Memorable
“In my AI in Business course, I studied how Netflix uses A/B testing to decide what artwork to show each user. I actually simulated one of those tests myself — ran the statistics, calculated significance, and identified where the prediction-decision gap creates real risk.”

The second answer is not more impressive because it sounds technical. It is more impressive because it is true and specific. You did that. You can talk about it for five minutes if asked. That credibility is what makes the difference.

2. What You Actually Know

Translating your coursework into interview language

Here is what you have studied this quarter, reframed the way a hiring manager would hear it.

EveryCure — Knowledge Graphs & AI Prediction. You understand how organizations use structured data to surface non-obvious patterns — in EveryCure’s case, finding drug-disease connections that human researchers would never find manually. In an interview, this translates to: understanding how AI can create value from existing data assets, how graph-based thinking differs from traditional data analysis, and why AI for social good is one of the most compelling frontiers in the field.

You can explain randomization, statistical significance, and the difference between a prediction and a decision. You actually ran a simulated A/B test. In an interview: “I’ve studied how Netflix uses controlled experiments to test every product change, and I’ve run my own simulation of that process.” That sentence will prompt follow-up questions rather than polite nodding.

Spotify — Recommendation Systems & Personalization. You understand collaborative filtering, why personalization is a competitive moat, and what happens when a recommendation system silently fails. You built a version of one yourself. In an interview, you can speak credibly about why personalization creates compounding business value and why the governance of AI systems matters as much as the accuracy.

Uber — Forecasting, Black-Box Models, and Responsible AI. You understand how an entire business can be operationalized through AI, what the prediction-decision gap means in practice, and what happens when algorithmic accountability fails. The Sydney hostage crisis example and the Upfront Fares case are not just stories — they are concrete examples of AI governance failures you can cite by name.

3. Three Questions You Will Likely Get

And how to answer them using what you know

Most interview questions about AI in business fall into one of three categories. Here is how to approach each one.

“Tell me about your experience with AI.”

Strong answer structure
Start with the course framing: “I took a course specifically on how companies use AI to make business decisions” — then name one or two specific things you did. The lab you built. The case you analyzed. The concept you can explain. End with a connection to the role you are interviewing for: “Given that this role involves [pricing / marketing / operations / analytics], the Uber forecasting and Spotify personalization cases were particularly relevant to me.”

“Can you give me an example of a data-driven decision?”

Strong answer structure
Use the Netflix A/B testing framework. Describe the decision (which thumbnail to show), the experiment (random assignment, control vs. treatment), the metric (click-through rate), and the outcome (statistically significant improvement). Then add: “What I found interesting was that even a small improvement in click-through rate compounds significantly at Netflix’s scale — that’s the core idea behind why data-driven experimentation is worth the investment.” That last sentence shows business thinking, not just technical knowledge.

“What do you think about AI — is it a risk or an opportunity?”

Strong answer structure
Do not give a one-sided answer. The most impressive response acknowledges both and shows you understand the nuance. “The research I’ve read shows that AI produces very different outcomes depending on how it’s deployed. A Stanford study (see Chapter 11) found that in occupations where AI augments workers — helping them do their jobs better — employment is actually growing. But in occupations where AI automates tasks entirely, entry-level hiring is declining. The risk isn’t AI itself. It’s whether organizations choose to use it to replace people or to make people better at what they do. That distinction is the single strongest predictor of whether AI leads to job growth or job loss in a given field.” That answer shows you have actually read the data, not just the headlines. Most interviewers will not have heard the augmentation-versus-automation framing, and it will land.

4. Your Group Project Is a Portfolio Piece

How to talk about what you built

Your group project — from problem proposal through system design to working prototype — is not a class assignment. It is the work. Think about what you actually did: you identified a real organization with a real problem, figured out what data would be needed, designed an AI system to support a specific decision, built a working prototype, and presented it as it evolved. Some of you built systems using the WWU Recreation Center as your context. Others used the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife. Others identified problems in local businesses, campus operations, or public agencies.

That is exactly what a junior analyst, associate product manager, or entry-level consultant would be asked to do in their first six months. The only difference is that you did it before you were hired. You already have the entry-level experience. You just need to frame it that way.

Do not say: “We did a group project where we built an AI thing.”

Say: “Our team identified a real operational problem at [the WWU Recreation Center / a state wildlife agency / a local business] and designed an AI system to address it. We defined the decision the system would support, designed the data pipeline, and built a working prototype. We presented it three times as it evolved.”

That is not a school project. That is a case study with a deliverable, a real client, and a working system. Interviewers in consulting, product management, operations, and marketing will recognize that process — because it is exactly how their teams work.

If they ask what problem you solved

Be ready to explain: (1) the organization and their business context, (2) the specific decision the system supported, (3) what data you used, (4) what the system produced, and (5) what value it created for that organization. That five-part structure is exactly how Milestone 2 asked you to think. You already have the answer — just practice saying it out loud.

5. Build a Portfolio Page

The portfolio is the experience

Here is the catch-22 every new graduate faces: you need experience to get hired, but you need to get hired to get experience. In a market where entry-level roles are being cut, where AI is automating the tasks that used to be the entry-level job, and where 300 people are applying for every open position through one-click-apply — a portfolio breaks the loop.

Most business school students — and university students generally — do not have one. Most cannot point an employer to anything they have built. That is your opening. When an AI is screening résumés and a recruiter is spending six seconds on each one, a URL that goes to real work you actually built is one of the few things that gets a human to look twice. The portfolio is not a supplement to your experience. In this market, the portfolio is the experience. It is proof that you can identify a problem, build something, and ship it — which is exactly what employers say they cannot find enough of.

And you already have the content. You built an AI prototype for a real organization. You ran A/B tests, built recommendation systems, trained models in Python. You have lab notebooks, project write-ups, and a working system you can demo. Most entry-level candidates spent four years writing papers that no one outside their professor ever read. You built things that real organizations can use. Put them somewhere a recruiter can see them.

The good news is that you do not need to know how to code a website. You can use Claude or another AI tool to build one for you — and the process of doing it is itself a demonstration of exactly the AI skills employers want to see.

Why this matters right now
Employers are receiving more applications than ever and spending less time on each one. A LinkedIn profile with a link to a real portfolio page that shows actual work — a lab write-up, a project summary, a notebook with results — is one of the few things that reliably gets a second look. It signals initiative, technical comfort, and the ability to communicate clearly. All three are things employers say they cannot find enough of.

Here is how to build one in a weekend, using tools you already have access to.

1
Decide what to include. Lead with your group prototype — that is your strongest piece. Include the organization you used as your context, the problem, the system you designed, and a screenshot or link to the working prototype. After that, add one or two lab write-ups (the recommendation system or the A/B test are usually the most impressive to employers). Two strong pieces are better than five mediocre ones.
2
Use Claude to build the page. Copy the prompt below into Claude. It will generate a complete, professional portfolio page as a single HTML file. Swap in your own details where you see the brackets. You do not need to understand every line of the code it produces — that is the whole point.
Ready-to-use prompt — paste this into Claude
Build me a clean, professional single-page portfolio website. I am a recent graduate of Western Washington University where I studied Management Information Systems. I took a course called MIS 432: AI in Business, where I studied how real companies use AI to make business decisions — and built working AI systems myself. About me: - Name: [Your name] - Degree and graduation year: [e.g., B.S. Business Administration, MIS concentration, 2026] - One or two sentences about your interests and career goals: [Write something genuine in your own voice] - LinkedIn URL: [Your LinkedIn] - Email: [Your email] Group prototype project (lead with this): - Organization we used as our context: [e.g., WWU Recreation Center, WA Dept. of Fish and Wildlife, etc.] - Problem we addressed: [One sentence describing the business problem] - What the system does: [One sentence describing what your AI prototype does] - My role on the team: [What you specifically contributed] - Link to the live prototype (if available): [URL] Lab projects (include 2-3 of these): - Lab 1: Built a knowledge graph for drug repurposing (EveryCure) — [link to your notebook if on GitHub] - Lab 2: Simulated a Netflix-style A/B test with statistical significance testing — [link] - Lab 3: Built a recommendation system using collaborative filtering (Spotify) — [link] - Lab 4: Built a demand forecasting model for ride-sharing (Uber) — [link] - Lab 5: Trained a deep learning image classifier (Waymo) — [link] - Lab 6: Built a marketplace matching and pricing system (Airbnb) — [link] - Lab 7: Built an AI agent for insurance claim appeals (Epic) — [link] [Delete any labs you did not complete or do not want to include.] Design instructions: - Single HTML file, no external dependencies - Clean, minimal, professional — not flashy - Mobile-friendly - Use a neutral color palette with one accent color - Include a short intro section, a featured project section (the group prototype), a lab projects section, and a contact section - Each project entry should have a title, a 2-3 sentence description, and a link - Do not use any stock images or placeholder content
Customize it

This prompt is a starting point. Add anything you want — other coursework, work experience, personal projects, volunteer work. Remove anything that does not feel like you. Ask Claude to revise the tone, change the layout, or adjust the color scheme. Iterate until it feels right. The portfolio should sound like you, not like a template.

3
Deploy it for free on GitHub Pages. This is exactly what your course website uses. Create a free GitHub account, upload your HTML file, turn on GitHub Pages in settings, and your site is live at a real URL in minutes. There are step-by-step guides on YouTube if you get stuck.
4
Write up your project clearly. Use AI to help you write a one-page summary of your prototype: what organization you used as your context, what problem you addressed, how the system worked, and what business value it would create. Keep it jargon-free. Imagine explaining it to a hiring manager, not a professor. The fact that you designed something for a real organization’s real problem — not a hypothetical case study — is the detail that makes this memorable.
5
Add the link everywhere — today, not later. Put it in your LinkedIn headline (the line right under your name that everyone sees first). Add it to the header of your resume, right next to your email and phone number. Put it in your email signature. When a recruiter or hiring manager sees a URL that goes to real work you actually built, it changes the conversation. You are no longer just another application in the pile. You are the candidate who showed up with evidence.
If you used synthetic data, that is fine
Some of your projects used mock or synthetic data rather than real production data. Do not apologize for that. Many data professionals actually encourage beginners to work with synthetic data — it lets you focus on learning the tools, the logic, and the design without getting stuck on data access issues that have nothing to do with your skills. The point of the prototype is that you learned how to think through a real problem, design a system, and build something that works. That is exactly what businesses are trying to do right now with AI — and most of them are further behind than you are. Be upfront about it: “We used synthetic data because we didn’t have access to production data, but the system is designed to work with real inputs.” That shows honesty and technical maturity, not weakness.
What to tell the interviewer

“I actually used AI to build my portfolio site — I described what I wanted, Claude wrote the HTML, and I deployed it on GitHub Pages. It took a few hours. I think that’s a pretty good example of how I think about using AI as a tool rather than just talking about it.” That answer, delivered naturally, will be remembered.

6. A Note on Honesty

Know what you know and know what you don’t

The biggest mistake students make when talking about AI in interviews is overclaiming. Do not say you “built a machine learning model” if you ran a simulation. Do not say you are “experienced in Python” if you wrote code with significant AI assistance. Interviewers who know the field will ask a follow-up question, and overclaiming collapses fast.

What you can say honestly is more than enough. You studied how real companies — a healthcare startup, a streaming giant, a music platform, a ride-sharing company — use AI to make decisions at scale. You ran experiments, built systems, and designed an AI product from scratch. You can explain the prediction-decision gap, Responsible AI and algorithmic accountability, A/B testing, and why black-box models create governance risks. That is a genuinely strong foundation, and it is all true.

Confidence in what you actually know is more impressive than bravado about things you don’t. The interviewer is not trying to catch you out — they are trying to find out whether you can think clearly and communicate well under pressure. You can.

Before Your Next Interview

Your AI Readiness Checklist

1.
Pick two case studies from this course you can talk about for two minutes each without notes.
2.

3.
Build a portfolio page and get it live at a real URL before you start applying.
4.
Practice the three interview questions in Section 3 out loud, not just in your head.
5.
Know your limits — be clear about what you built vs. what you studied vs. what you are still learning.
6.
Add your portfolio URL to your LinkedIn, resume, and email signature today, not the night before an interview.

The job market is harder. The data confirms it, and you should not let anyone tell you otherwise. But most of your competition is going into interviews with generic answers and nothing to show. You have specific knowledge, real work, a portfolio that proves you can build things, and now a framework for talking about all of it. That combination is rarer than you think — and in a market where AI is screening out hundreds of applicants before a human even looks, being the candidate who shows up with real work at a real URL is one of the most concrete things you can do to get through the door.
