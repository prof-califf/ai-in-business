Part 1A

Conceptual Understanding

35 points

Answer each question in one well-developed paragraph (five sentences minimum). These are not definition questions — they ask you to use a concept from the case to reason through a problem, make a judgment, or argue a position. Make sure you are applying the course vocabulary throughout your answers — if the chapter calls it a "prediction–decision gap" or "algotorial," use those terms precisely.

Tying in material across chapters shows critical thinking. If a concept from the Uber case helps you make a stronger argument about Spotify, use it. The cases are connected — your answers should be too.

A definition earns partial credit at best. Full marks go to answers that take a position, use case evidence to support it, and acknowledge a complication or trade-off. If your answer could have been written by someone who only read a summary, it hasn't gone deep enough. Use external sources — news articles, company blog posts, academic papers, industry reports — alongside the chapter material to strengthen your arguments. Include URLs for any sources you reference.

confidence = 0.85

1B.2
A/B test result checker (Netflix)
2.5 pts
control_clicks = 120
control_users = 1000
treatment_clicks = 155
treatment_users = 1000
control_rate = control_clicks / control_users
treatment_rate = treatment_clicks / treatment_users
lift = treatment_rate - control_rate
if lift > 0.02:
    print("Ship it")
else:
    print("Not enough difference")

(1) Netflix runs A/B tests to compare two versions of something. What does this code calculate for each group, and what does it print? What does "lift" mean here?

(2) Your team wants to be more cautious — they only want to ship changes that show at least a 5 percentage point improvement. What would you change? Also: in 1–2 sentences, why is this code alone not enough to decide whether to ship? (Hint: think about what the chapter says about statistical significance.)

Skip rate calculator (Spotify)
2.5 pts

skips = 140
replays = 15

replay_rate = replays / total_plays

(1) Spotify uses implicit signals like skips and replays to decide what to recommend. What does this code calculate, and what does it print? Would this song keep getting recommended?

(2) In 1–2 sentences: the chapter talks about explore vs. exploit. This code is pure exploit — it stops recommending anything with a high skip rate. What's the risk of that? What kind of music would never get a chance?

This part tests everything: can you think like a product manager, build with GenAI, and reason like someone who takes responsibility for what they ship?

What "business case" means

A business case is not "AI is cool and this would be neat." It is an argument: here is a specific problem, here is who has it, here is how GenAI solves it better than the alternative, and here is what makes it worth building. Think like someone pitching to a skeptical VP who controls the budget.

Prompting tip

You will use GenAI to build your prototype in 3b — and the quality of what it produces depends on how well you prompt it. Give it your specific scenario, your target user, the functionality you need, and the constraints (single HTML file, no backend). Iterate: if the first output isn't right, tell it what to fix and why. Review the Writing Good Prompts guide — especially the sections on context, intent, and iteration — before you start building.

What a strong submission looks like

This is your chance to really dive into working with GenAI — use it, experiment with it, build with it, and then think critically about what you built. The best exams apply course concepts throughout — not as definitions, but as tools for thinking about AI in business. Strong Part 1 answers use vocabulary like "prediction–decision gap" and "explore vs. exploit" to build real arguments, not just to show you memorized the terms. Strong Part 2 submissions show a working prototype that you clearly engaged with — you tweaked it, you understand what it does, and your written analysis identifies a real limitation and connects it to a specific chapter. Strong Part 3 business cases make a clear argument for why your prototype needs to exist, name the specific user it serves, and honestly address what could go wrong. Strong Part 4 reflections go beyond "AI uses a lot of energy" — they connect your own calculator results to the chapter's argument about inference, waste, and disclosure. Throughout the exam, specificity wins: "My symptom checker could cause harm if someone with chest pain dismisses the pain because the tool suggested they wait and see" earns more than "the AI could give bad advice."
